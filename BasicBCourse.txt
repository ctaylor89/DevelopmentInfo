What is the purpose of the accounting equation?

The accounting equation is a simple, yet powerful, concept that helps us understand how a business's resources are financed.
The accounting equation is expressed as: Assets = Liabilities + Equity
The accounting equation is the foundation of the balance sheet. A balance sheet is the accounting equation displayed as a 
financial statement. 
---
Taking your time to fully understand the accounting equation will help you:
	explain why events happen the way they do in the accounting process, 
	communicate the reasons behind financial decisions or discrepancies,
	and, spot errors or inconsistencies in your accounting software.

Assets are the valuable resources a business owns. Cash, accounts receivable, inventory, investments in other businesses, 
equipment, intellectual property (such as a patent)

Liabilities are the obligations the business owes to others. Liabilities can be loans to support the business, mortgage, payroll,
sales tax owed to the government, credit cards, or any other debts that need to be repaid. They represent the business's 
promises to others.

When you look at a balance sheet, you see how the business's assets are financed. Liabilities and equity provide the fuel to 
acquire those assets. The balance sheet shows the relationship between what the business owns, what it owes, and how much the 
owners have invested.
---
Equity = Assets - Liabilities
Which items represent equity in the accounting equation? 
A: Investments made by the owners and retained earnings. Equity represents the residual interest in the business's assets after 
deducting liabilities. Equity includes the owner's investments and accumulated profits or losses.
---
Revenue represents the income your client earns through their business operations. It's their gross proceeds from sales, whether 
that's products, services, rentals, or anything else that bring money into the business.

Expenses represent the costs of doing business, such as labor costs like salaries and employee benefits, operating costs like 
utilities, rent and insurance, and other essential expenses like taxes and advertising, or anything else that keeps their 
business moving.

Revenue and expenses come together on the income statement(P&L), a document that summarizes a client's financial  performance 
through the calculation of revenue minus expenses. This calculation helps you determine your client's net income or net loss 
for specified time periods.

Note: In accounting you will often find negative values noted by being shown in parenthesis, for example: ($6,000).

Debits and credits 
Debits and credits are the choreography of your client's financial performance. Understanding their movements helps you keep 
track of your client's assets, expenses, liabilities, owner's equity, and revenue.

Debits
Debits represent an increase in assets or expenses, or a decrease in liabilities, owner’s equity, or revenue.

Credits
Credits represent a decrease in assets, or expenses, or an increase in liabilities, owner's equity, or revenue.

The cardinal rule of bookkeeping: debits and credits need to be equal. It's like a balancing act where the numbers on both sides of the stage should always match up.

When recording the purchase of office supplies purchased on account, which side of the ledger should the accounts payable be 
recorded on?  A:Credit. Accounts payable is a liability, and when you purchase office supplies on account, it increases your
liabilities. Therefore, you would record the increase in accounts payable on the credit side of the ledger.

When recording the payment of salaries to employees, which side of the salary expense ledger should the transaction be recorded?
A:Debit. Salary expense is an expense account, and when you pay salaries to employees, it increases your expenses. Therefore, 
you would record the increase in salary expense on the debit side of the ledger.

Accounting principles play an important role in maintaining financial integrity and ensuring accurate reporting in business. 
Accounting principles also provide a standardized framework that guides financial information recording, analysis, and 
communication. 

Let's explore the first four accounting principles to see how they will guide the integrity of your bookkeeping practices.

1) The Economic Entity assumption is crucial for accurate financial reporting, as it ensures that the business's financial 
statements reflect only its activities and not those of its owners or other businesses.

2) Reliability is a fundamental principle in accounting that emphasizes the importance of recording financial transactions 
based on verifiable and objective evidence. The Reliability principle ensures that the information you record in backing your 
client's financial documents is verifiable and backed by proper documentation. If your client can't provide an invoice, receipt, 
or bank statement to support a transaction, it is not a reliable transaction and can't be recorded.
---
3) The Full Disclosure principle is a fundamental accounting principle that emphasizes the importance of providing all relevant 
information in financial statements. This principle ensures that stakeholders, such as investors, creditors, and regulators, have 
access to complete and transparent information about a company's financial position, performance, and any potential risks or 
uncertainties.

For example, if a worker files a worker's compensation claim, it's essential to include that information in the notes of the 
financial statements.
---
4) The Conservatism principle is a fundamental accounting principle that emphasizes the importance of exercising caution and 
prudence when making financial decisions and reporting financial information. It encourages accountants to recognize potential 
losses or liabilities as soon as they are reasonably foreseeable, while being more conservative in recognizing potential gains 
or assets.

The Conservatism Assumption directs bookkeepers to exercise caution and choose options that show potential losses while 
excluding potential gains in uncertain situations.
---
Let's explore four more accounting principles that will help you maintain integrity and accuracy as a bookkeeper.

1) The Materiality principle states: An accountant does not have to follow every accounting rule perfectly for items that are so 
small or insignificant that they would not affect a reasonable person's understanding of the financial statements.

In other words: Accountants focus their time and effort on information that could influence decisions.
A transaction is considered material if leaving it out, recording it incorrectly, or presenting it differently could change 
how someone views the company’s financial position or performance. Materiality allows accountants to use professional judgment.

Materiality does not mean "ignore small transactions."
It means: Record and present information in a way that is accurate enough that financial statement users are not misled.

Use caution when applying this principle. Determining materiality can be highly subjective. When in doubt, seek advice from 
colleagues or an accountant who can provide valuable insights.
---
2) The Consistency principle is another key accounting principle that emphasizes the importance of using the same accounting 
methods and practices over time. This principle ensures that financial statements are comparable across periods, allowing 
stakeholders to identify trends and make informed decisions.

Once a business adopts a specific accounting method for recording certain items, it commits to entering all similar items in the 
same way in the future. This principle applies to line items on all financial statements and reports. The Consistency principle 
also helps to build trust and credibility with stakeholders, as it demonstrates a commitment to transparency and reliability in 
financial reporting.
---
3) The Monetary Unit assumption is a fundamental accounting principle that assumes that the currency used in financial reporting 
remains stable over time. This principle allows accountants to record and report financial transactions in a consistent and 
meaningful manner, facilitating accurate comparisons and analysis of financial information across different periods.

It states that you use one currency throughout all of your accounting activities. If your client purchased a piece of land and 
the value has increased over time, you don't need to adjust the recorded value based on the current market price. Just stick to 
the price paid at the time of purchase. 
---
4) The Going Concern principle is a fundamental accounting principle that assumes a business will continue to operate indefinitely, 
unless there is evidence to the contrary. This principle is essential for financial reporting, as it allows accountants to prepare 
financial statements under the assumption that the business will remain in operation for the foreseeable future. This assumption 
affects the valuation of assets and liabilities, as well as the recognition of revenues and expenses.

If and when a business is in danger of failing and is no longer considered a Going Concern, it's essential to report the issues 
it is facing, such as ongoing losses, credit denials, or lawsuits. Transparency is critical in these situations.

Accounting Cycles are the processes that businesses use to record and process financial transactions. The accounting cycle is a 
series of steps that businesses follow to ensure accurate and complete financial reporting.

Simply put, the accounting cycle is the journey from the beginning of a transaction to its inclusion in the financial statements.
The accounting cycle is a process that includes six steps.
---
I am a bookkeeper. My job is to help clients stay informed about their business by providing accurate and current financial 
information. Today, I'd like to talk to you about the accounting cycle.

The accounting cycle is a process that includes six steps that help ensure everything is done properly. It starts when a 
business makes a transaction and continues until the financial statements are produced at the end of an accounting period.

By following these steps, I can generate accurate financial statements and close the books with confidence.

Analyz transactions and gathering supporting documents
1) In the first step, I collect and analyze events to see if they are transactions that impact the business. Transactions can be 
things like business purchases, paying off debts, or earning revenue from sales. Then I gather the source documents that back 
up those transactions. 

2) The next step is to record and post all the important information about each transaction. I do this by recording journal entries.
In double-entry accounting, every transaction is recorded as both a debit and a credit in two or more subledger accounts.

Once these journal entries are written and approved, I transfer them as summary entries, or “post” them to the general ledger, 
or GL. The general ledger is like a master record that summarizes all the financial transactions of the business. It contains 
all accounts and financial records necessary to prepare the financial statements. 

3) Then, it’s time for me to prepare the unadjusted trial balance. This happens at the end of each period. The recorded transactions 
are combined into a trial balance. This is the “unadjusted trial balance" as it is prepared before making any adjusting entries. 
The unadjusted trial balance provides an initial overview of financial information and helps me catch any mistakes made earlier 
in the accounting cycle. The unadjusted trial balance is considered accurate if the total debit account balances equal total 
credit account balances.

4) Next, I prepare adjusting entries to make sure the financial statements are accurate. Usually, it's the job of a CPA or accountant 
to let me know which adjusting entries I need to make.

Adjusting entries are like fine-tuning the financial statements to accurately reflect how the business is doing. They help 
ensure that the information included in the statements shows an accurate picture by making sure that revenue and expenses match 
up within the right accounting periods.

5) After making the adjusting entries, it’s time for me to prepare the adjusted trial balance. This is a list of the final balances 
in all accounts. It shows that all the changes and corrections have been accurately recorded and the accounts are in balance. 
In other words, everything has been properly updated and the financial records are accurate.

An Adjusted Trial Balance is a trial balance after all adjusting journal entries have been entered, but before the financial 
statements are finalized. It is one of the last steps in the accounting cycle.

As a bookkeeper, you typically prepare an adjusted trial balance after completing month-end or year-end adjustments, such as:
Depreciation, Accrued expenses, Accrued revenue, Prepaid expense adjustments, Unearned revenue adjustments, 
Inventory adjustments (if applicable).

QuickBooks Online does not include a report specifically named "Adjusted Trial Balance." Instead, once all adjusting entries 
have been posted, the standard Trial Balance report becomes your adjusted trial balance because it reflects the updated balances.

6) Finally, I use the adjusted trial balance to prepare the financial statements. There are four main types of financial statements:
	The income statement, the balance sheet, the statement of equity and the statement of cash flows

Financial statements are used to analyze the business’s performance, understand its cash flow, and evaluate its overall financial
health. By following these steps, I make sure I accurately record transactions, fix mistakes, and balance the books.

Periodicity is a fundamental accounting principle that emphasizes the importance of dividing a business's financial activities 
into specific time periods for reporting purposes. This principle allows businesses to provide timely and relevant financial 
information to stakeholders, enabling them to make informed decisions based on the company's performance during those periods.

Revenue Recongition is a fundamental accounting principle that determines when revenue should be recognized and recorded in the 
financial statements. It ensures that revenue is reported in the appropriate accounting period, reflecting the actual economic 
activity of the business. According to the revenue recognition principle, a business should recognize its revenue only when a 
good or service has been provided to the customer. If a customer paid in advance, but the good or service has not been provided 
yet, then the revenue should not be recognized.

A business recognizes its revenues when the goods or services are provided to the customer, regardless of when the payment is 
received. The key idea is that revenue gets recognized when the work is finished and delivered, not when the payment arrives. 
But on the flip side, if the work isn't complete and the money arrives early, that is money owed rather than revenue until the 
work is finished or the goods are delivered. 

Matching principle is a fundamental accounting principle that requires expenses to be recognized and recorded in the same 
accounting period as the revenues they help generate. This ensures that the financial statements accurately reflect the 
company's profitability during a specific period. So you would recognize the expense of the materials used to make a product in 
the same period as the revenue from selling that product even it was paid for in the next accounting period.

Advantages and limitations of dedicated software

Accounting software handles proper crediting and debiting based on the transaction type, making sure that all credits and debits 
are balanced.

Advantages:
1. Automation: Reduces manual data entry and the risk of human error.
2. Real-time reporting: Provides up-to-date financial information.
3. Compliance: Helps ensure adherence to accounting standards and regulations.

Disadvantages:
1. Cost: Dedicated software can be expensive to purchase and maintain.
2. Complexity: Some software may have a steep learning curve or require specialized training.
3. Integration: Difficulty in integrating with existing systems or data sources.

Whenever you enter a transaction, accounting software quickly updates all the relevant accounts at once. That means you don't 
have to manually calculate balances or transfer data between different accounts, which is a real time saver!

Accounting software won't stop you from accidentally categorizing transactions the wrong way or typing in the wrong amounts.

No matter how advanced the software is, it can't replace the expertise and knowledge of a skilled bookkeeper who understands how 
to organize accounts, how different transactions affect the books, and how to help clients understand complex financial statements.

Remember: To earn the trust and satisfaction of your clients, it's important to follow the accounting cycle process.

Chart of accounts and general ledger. How are these different than a transaction journal?

The chart of accounts lists all of the accounts and sub-accounts used to categorize transactions.

A transaction journal, or "journal", is used to list the details of an individual event, like an expense or revenue transaction. 
The transaction journal, lists transactions in chronological order and provides a detailed record of all transactions.
Listing a transaction in the journal makes it easier to see than searching through the general ledger.

The general ledger groups transactions by account type and shows the balances for each account. The general ledger is used to 
prepare financial statements and other financial reports.

The transaction journal, general ledger, and chart of accounts all show a business's financial information in a different way, 
but they all are connected and impacted by each transaction.

Cash-basis accounting is a method where revenues and expenses are recognized based on actual receipt or payment, rather than the 
completion of work or delivery of goods. This means transactions are recorded in real-time as they occur. 
Here are some key points about cash basis accounting:
• Does not have accounts payable or accounts receivable
• Everything is recorded when it happens
• Cannot be used if you offer credit or have inventory

Because of it's simplicity, cash-basis accounting is a favorite of small businesses. However, because you aren't tracking future 
transactions, it doesn't always give a full picture of income and expenses; it's more of a snapshot of the business activity. 

When using cash-basis accounting, you may not have a clear understanding of your business's financial health, especially if you 
have outstanding invoices or bills that haven't been paid yet.

When using cash-basis accounting, you only have to pay taxes on the money you have received. This can be a big advantage for 
small businesses that are just starting out and may not have a lot of cash flow.
---
Accrual accounting is the method that is required by GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles). This means that businesses 
that use accrual accounting are more likely to be compliant with accounting regulations. 

Modified cash-basis accounting is a hybrid approach that combines elements of both cash-basis and accrual accounting.

Modified cash-basis accounting is a practical and widely used accounting method among small businesses. This approach combines 
the best of cash-basis and accrual accounting to help businesses stay on top of their finances.

Modified cash-basis accounting recognizes revenue when payment is received but expenses when they are incurred. By incorporating 
some elements of accrual accounting, modified cash-basis accounting allows businesses to have a more comprehensive view of their 
financial standind.

In modified cash-basis accounting, we introduce two important accounts: accounts payable and accounts receivable.
If a business has prepaid expense accounts, this method may not be suitable.

It recognizes revenue when payment is received but expenses when incurred.

Step 4: Prepare adjusted entries

At the end of the accounting period, bookkeepers go through the process of making adjusting entries. This involves analyzing the 
unadjusted trial balance and performing necessary updates by recording adjusting entries. These entries, which include deferrals, 
accruals, and tax adjustments, expenses incurred but not recorded, can impact net income, the balance sheet, and owner's equity.

1. Review all accounts for necessary adjustments.
2. Make journal entries to record adjustments.
3. Update the general ledger.
4. Prepare an adjusted trial balance.

To ensure accurate financial records, you will record adjusting entries in both the journal and the ledger.

To ensure that financial statements reflect all revenues earned and expenses incurred during the accounting period, adjusting 
entries are made on the last day of an accounting period. Adjusting entries are necessary to include business transactions 
that are not yet recorded in the accounting records.

The entries made include capturing transactions that have not yet been recorded on the books that belong in the accounting 
period, such as expenses incurred but not yet paid or recorded, revenue that was received in advance of delivering goods or 
services, and adjusting for prepayments of expenses, such as insurance premiums.

Tax adjustments are also commonly made once per year as part of adjusting entries. The process of making adjusting entries, 
also known as adjusting journal entries, involves analyzing the current account balance, determining the desired balance for the 
accounting period’s end, and recording the adjusting entry.

Usually, an accountant or Certified Public Accountant will handle the preparation of adjustments. As a bookkeeper, your role will 
be to implement the recommended adjustments by recording the adjusting journal entries.

Types of adjustments

When using accrual accounting, adjusting entries help you apply the matching principle, which means you match expenses with 
revenues in the period when they occur, not when cash is exchanged. Adjusting entries play a key role in aligning expenses to 
revenues within the accounting period in accrual accounting.

Record revenues when they are earned, regardless of when the business receives the money. 
Record expenses when they are incurred, regardless of when the business pays them.

Adjusting entries reflect the actual exchange of money and modify real-time entries to match revenue and expenses in the period 
they occur. They include various types of entries. Deferrals remove transactions that should be assigned to a different time 
period. For example, if a business buys a new lawn mower but hasn't used it yet, none of its value should be attributed to the 
revenue earned in the current period.

Similarly, if a client pays in advance for a job that hasn't been completed, the revenue should be adjusted to match the actual 
completion of the job. Overall, deferrals ensure that revenues and expenses are properly matched to the relevant time period.


Accruals, on the other hand, are the opposite of deferrals. Accruals are transactions that include future payments or expenses. 
Accruals recognize revenues and expenses when they are incurred, regardless of when cash is exchanged. This means that if a 
business provides a service in one accounting period but doesn't receive payment until the next period, the revenue is still 
recognized in the period the service was provided.

If the expected life of the vehicle is 5 years, $10,000 would be recognized as an expense each year the vehicle is used until 
the full value of the vehicle has been expensed. This gradual recognition of expenses reduces the immediate impact on net profit, 
as only a portion of the cost is recorded as an expense in each period.

Depreciation is a form of tax adjustment that a evenly spreads out the cost of an asset over its expected lifespan.

Your client provided a service to a customer and they have yet to pay. The accounting period is ending today. Which type of 
journal entry will need to be made?  A: An accrual journal entry. Since the service has been provided but payment has not yet 
been received, the revenue needs to be recognized in the current accounting period. This will involve debiting Accounts 
Receivable and crediting Service Revenue to reflect the earned revenue and the amount owed by the customer.

A customer paid in advance for a service. The work has not yet been started and the accounting period has come to an end. Which 
type of journal entry will need to be made? A: A deferral journal entry. Since the customer has paid in advance for a service 
that has not yet been provided, the revenue cannot be recognized in the current accounting period. This will involve debiting 
Cash and crediting Unearned Revenue to reflect the cash received and the liability owed to the customer for future services.

What is the depreciation of a vehicle entered in the journal entries?  A: Depreciation is typically recorded as a journal entry 
at the end of each accounting period to allocate the cost of the vehicle over its useful life. The journal entry would involve 
debiting Depreciation Expense and crediting Accumulated Depreciation.
Note: The practice answer is "A Tax Adjustment journal entry". Depreciation is a common tax adjustment.

Adjustments should always be marked so that it is clear which accounts were affected by the transaction and by how much.

Step 6 - Prepare financial statements

The income (P&L) statement provides a snapshot of a business's financial performance during a specific time period. It includes the 
total revenue, which is all of the income generated from sales of goods and services provided. It accounts for the total cost 
of goods sold, which represents the total expenses directly associated with producing the goods or services that generated the 
revenue. It calculates the gross profit, which is the result of total revenue minus cost of goods sold. 

The income statement(P&L) also includes any other business expenses incurred during the reporting period, such as rent, salaries, and 
advertising costs. 

By subtracting the total expenses–including taxes–from the revenue, the income statement reveals the resulting net profit or 
loss for the business.

The income statement is a crucial tool for stakeholders, including management, investors, and creditors, as it provides insights 
into the company's profitability and operational efficiency. By analyzing the income statement, stakeholders can make informed 
decisions about resource allocation, investment opportunities, and overall business strategy.

Gross Profit minus Operating Expenses equals Net Income.
Gross Profit minus Operating Expenses minus Taxes equals Net Income.

Total revenue is the same as total income. Total expenses are the same as total costs. Net income is the same as net profit

In the statement of cash flows, is the purpose of this statement to show how much cash moves in both directions, or is the 
purpose of the statement to show the resulting balance after all cash movements?

A: The true purpose of the Statement of Cash Flows is to show how much cash moves in both directions—meaning it tracks the 
actual inflows (sources) and outflows (uses) of cash over a specific period of time. While it does show the resulting final 
balance at the very bottom, that ending balance isn't the primary focus. In fact, you can already find the exact resulting cash 
balance just by looking at the "Cash and Cash Equivalents" line on the Balance Sheet.

Instead, the Statement of Cash Flows breaks down how you arrived at that balance by organizing the movements into three 
distinct directions:
	Operating Activities: Cash generated or spent from day-to-day core business operations (e.g., cash collected from customers, 
	                      cash paid for bills/inventory).

	Investing Activities: Cash spent or received from long-term assets (e.g., buying equipment, selling property).
	Financing Activities: Cash moving between the business and its owners or creditors (e.g., taking out a loan, owner draws, 
	                      or paying back principal).

In short: The Balance Sheet shows the resulting balance, while the Statement of Cash Flows tells the story of the movement that 
happened to get there.

and depreciates it over its useful life. The lease payments are split into interest and principal components, similar to a loan.

Is COGS calculated at the time the related products are sold?  Yes, under accrual accounting, Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) is

When a customer purchases the inventory, two things happen simultaneously:

	B) COGS is recognized for the cost of that inventory, moving the item's cost off the Balance Sheet (crediting Inventory) and 
	onto the Income Statement (debiting COGS).

Inventory valuation refers to the process of assigning a cost to the inventory items listed on the balance sheet. 

The inventory valuation method chosen by a business directly affects how the value of inventory is calculated and the amount 
debited to COGS when inventory is sold. The inventory valuation method also impacts the financial statements, including the 
balance sheet and income statement, as well as tax calculations and cash flow management.

In accounting, there's a method called FIFO that helps businesses keep track of their inventory. FIFO means that the first 
things purchased are the first things sold. They are sold at the cost of the oldest inventory, which is recorded as COGS on the 
income statement. The remaining inventory is valued at the cost of the most recent purchases, which is recorded as an asset on 
the balance sheet.

Another method is LIFO, which means that the last things purchased are the first things sold. They are sold at the cost of the 
most recent inventory, which is recorded as COGS on the income statement. The remaining inventory is valued at the cost of the 
oldest purchases, which is recorded as an asset on the balance sheet.

The average cost method is another way businesses determine the value of their inventory. They calculate the average cost by 
adding up the prices of all the things they bought and dividing it by the total number of items. Many small businesses like 
using this method because it's simple and easy to calculate.

As a bookkeeper, it's important to ensure consistency when applying the chosen inventory valuation method.

If a client decides to change costing methods of their inventory, that is not a quick change. There are forms that you or your 
client will have to file with the government to alert of that change, because their tax calculations may adjust.
