If I write a check for $200 to pay down a loan and enter the transaction into the journal, what would the journal entry be?

When you write a check to pay down a loan, you are reducing both what you owe (a liability) and your cash balance (an asset).
Assuming this payment is going strictly toward the principal balance of the loan, the standard journal entry would be:
The Journal Entry
	Debit: Loan Payable (Liability account) — $200
	Credit: Checking Account / Cash (Asset account) — $200

Why this works (The Accounting Logic):
Debiting the Loan Payable account decreases your liabilities. Since liabilities naturally have a credit balance, a debit lowers 
the total amount you owe to the lender.

Crediting the Checking account decreases your assets. Since assets naturally have a debit balance, a credit reflects money 
moving out of your bank account.

Note on Interest: If that $200 check also includes an interest fee (for example, $180 goes to principal and $20 goes to interest),
you would split the debit side like this:
	Debit Loan Payable: $180
	Debit Interest Expense: $20
	Credit Checking Account: $200
