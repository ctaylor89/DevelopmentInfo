Note: In QuickBooks Online 2025, the old “App Transactions” feature has effectively been folded into the broader Banking 
(Transactions) center. There isn’t a one-to-one renamed feature. Instead, QuickBooks redesigned the workflow:
👉 Connected apps now feed transactions directly into the Transactions (Banking) tab, alongside bank feeds.

If you are used to the old setup:
	Old: App Transactions = separate place to manage app data
	New: App data = just another feed inside the Transactions center
If you don’t see “App transactions,” go to Bank transactions

The old QB setup and the new QB setup are different, but the core functionality of managing transactions from connected apps 
still exists — it’s just integrated into the main banking workflow now.

Bank Transactions (formerly Banking) is the hub for managing all transactions that come in from connected bank accounts, credit cards,
and apps. It’s where you review, categorize, and match transactions to keep your books accurate and up to date.

On the Transactions page, the available tabs include: Bank Transactions, Rules, Reconcile. Each tab serves a specific purpose 
in the banking workflow, from managing incoming transactions to setting up rules for automation and reconciling accounts.

Rules in QuickBooks Online allow you to automate the categorization of transactions that come in through your bank feeds. By 
setting up rules, you can save time and ensure consistency in how transactions are categorized.

As you manage your bank feeds, you may encounter common reconciliation issues such as:
	- Duplicate transactions
	- Missing transactions
	- Incorrect transaction amounts

As you manage the bank feed, you are deciding how to categorize transactions, which accounts to match them to, and whether to 
add them as new transactions. This is a critical step in ensuring that the financial data in QuickBooks is accurate and up to 
date, which is essential for generating reliable financial reports and making informed business decisions.
---
Overview of setup tasks

1. Connecting accounts
By connecting bank and credit card accounts to QuickBooks, you can streamline your accounting process and save valuable time 
that would otherwise be spent on entering transactions manually. When accounts are connected, transactions come into QuickBooks 
daily through the bank feed.

2. Creating bank rules
When transactions come in via the bank feed, you need to categorize them so they are allocated to the appropriate accounts in 
the chart of accounts. Bank rules speed up this process and increase consistency. You can create rules that automatically 
categorize transactions for you.

To sign into the bank or credit card account via QuickBooks, start from Bank transactions and select Connect account, if this is 
the first account to be connected. Otherwise select Link account.

It’s best practice for clients to sign into their account themselves. Note that for some banks, this will need to be with the 
primary account holder’s user credentials. Some clients will try to give you full access to their bank/credit card account by 
sharing their passwords with you. You should never do this.

Each bank or credit card account to be connected needs to have an equivalent account in the chart of accounts. If there isn’t 
one, add the account now.

Once the client has been verified, the account will connect and transactions will be pulled into QuickBooks. In most cases, the 
opening balance will automatically populate with the total amount of whatever is already in the bank account. For more accurate 
records, it’s a good idea to find out more about this opening balance—what is it and how did it get there? For example, if it’s 
a new business and the owner contributed their own money, the opening balance should be recorded as owner’s equity.

Manually upload transactions
When connecting accounts, your client will be asked to select the date range they want to import transactions from, and each 
bank has its own rules on how far back data can be imported. If you want to import further back than the bank allows, you can 
do it manually in bulk by importing a .csv file. This can be done for both newly connected accounts and existing linked accounts.

QuickBooks Checking is a bank account for small businesses, and once your client has applied and been approved, QuickBooks 
Checking automatically syncs transactions to QuickBooks. This streamlines the process of managing finances by reducing the need 
for manual entry and reconciliation.

The List settings gear enables you to modify the settings of the Bank transactions list to tailor how the data is displayed. 
These preferences are saved per client so that you can tailor workflows to their unique needs.

Displaying the Payee is switched on by default. It’s a good idea to keep it this way so that you can check that a payee is 
assigned to all transactions. When transactions are assigned a payee, they appear in the Vendor list. So if your client asks how 
much they’ve spent with a particular vendor, you can see this information easily, rather than having to rely on the general 
ledger. Having a payee assigned is also helpful later for potential 1099 processing. You can view transactions without a vendor 
in the Books review.

Turning on grouping is a key way to accelerate the workflow for managing the bank feed.

Editable dates are especially useful for clients who write checks. Perhaps a check clears on the 1st of the month, but was 
written in the previous month. It may be helpful to edit the date and put it to the previous month, so that the transaction is 
recorded in the actual month it was written, but use caution as editing dates can throw off reconciliation.

Copy bank detail to memo
Copy bank detail to memo is turned on by default and it’s strongly advised to keep it that way. It means the full information 
from the bank about the transaction is recorded in QuickBooks. In the Description column, QuickBooks cleans up the bank detail 
to make it easier to read, and it can be overwritten. By including the bank detail in the memo, you create an audit trail that 
can be checked by running a general ledger detail report. As you’ll see later, the bank detail is referred to as bank text when 
setting up rules.
---
Creating bank rules
The second and final setup task for banking is to create bank rules. When transactions are downloaded via the bank feed, if they 
are not matched to existing transactions in QuickBooks, you need to categorize them so they go into the appropriate accounts. 
Creating bank rules helps automate this process, including assigning the transaction type, category, payee, and—if the client 
uses these categorization methods—class, project, and location. This saves a lot of time and ensures consistency of categorization.

Bank Rules have two elements: conditions and actions.
--
Conditions:
Bank Rule conditions define which transactions to look for in the bank feeds, and can include criteria such as a specific bank 
account, the keyword in the description, or a transaction amount. You can add up to five conditions per rule.

Actions tell QuickBooks what to do with transactions that meet the rule conditions. Examples of these instructions include 
automatically posting a transaction to the correct account(s) and/or assigning prespecified data such as payee, category, class, 
location, and/or project.

Be aware that if you make a rule specific to a particular account, if a client then changes their account details, the rule 
won’t be carried over.

You can specify whether the bank activity must meet Any or All of the specified conditions before QuickBooks applies the rule.

You can choose whether the rule should identify transactions based on a specified Description, Bank text, or Amount.

Bank text (also known as bank detail), is the transaction detail that QuickBooks receives from the bank. Depending on the bank, 
this may include information such as the store number, city, state, phone number, and date. It might also include 
hard-to-interpret abbreviations of vendor/customer names. To see these details, expand the transaction in the bank feed.

Description is QuickBooks shortened and cleaned-up interpretation of the bank text.
It’s often more useful to create a rule with conditions referencing the bank text than the description because a bank text rule 
will look at the bank detail and the description text, whereas a description rule will only look at the description text.
--
Actions:
By choosing Assign, you can instruct the rule to assign the values Assign or Exclude to the following fields: Payee, Category, 
Class, Location, and Project. Assign means that when a transaction meets the conditions of the rule, QuickBooks will 
automatically fill in the specified value for that field. Exclude means that when a transaction meets the conditions of the rule, 
QuickBooks will ensure that the specified value is not assigned to that field.

When you set up the actions for a bank rule, you can specify how you want QuickBooks to categorize transactions that meet the
conditions of the rule. You can specify the transaction type (Expense, Transfer, Check, or Credit Card Payment), the category 
(which is the account in the chart of accounts), the payee, and any other relevant details. These transaction options will be 
very depending on the conditions you set up for the rule. For example, if you set a condition that looks for transactions with a 
specific payee, the only transaction type options you will have in the actions are Expense and Check, because it wouldn’t make 
sense to have a rule that looks for a specific payee and then categorizes the transaction as a transfer or credit card payment.

For money-in transactions, best practice is to integrate third-party apps as much as possible, and this means rules aren’t 
needed. However, it’s not always possible, and so, when using rules for money-in transactions, the options are:
Deposit, Credit card payment, Transfer

Selecting the arrow next to Select a category allows you to select the account on the chart of accounts to categorize this 
transaction.

If the transaction needs to use more than one account, select "+ Add a split" to split the amount of the transaction by 
percentage or amount. You might want to do this, for example, if a phone bill is made up partly of payment for a new handset, 
which could go to Equipment, and the rest could go to Services. Another example is if a payment from a customer includes payment 
for both an invoice and a retainer, you could split the transaction so that the invoice payment is categorized as Accounts 
Receivable and the retainer is categorized as a liability account such as Unearned Revenue.

Setting the payee is optional, but recommended. If the rule applies to money-in transactions, you can use the Payee dropdown to 
select the customer. For money-out transactions, you can use this dropdown to select a vendor.

--
If the rule applies to money-out transactions and you have enabled the setting to Track expenses and items by client (only 
available on Plus and Advanced plans), a Payee and Customer dropdown will be displayed. Otherwise, they will just have the Payee 
field. The Payee field denotes whom you paid; the Customer field denotes who it was for. For example, if your client bought 
supplies for their customer, then the supply store is the payee, and your client’s customer is the customer.

If the client is recording a cost and wants it assigned to a particular project, sub-customer, or customer, they can select them 
from the Customer dropdown. This allows them to see it on the profit and loss by customer report.
--
You can also create a memo that’ll be added to transactions. To do this, select + Assign more and a Replace bank memo field will 
be displayed. You can specify whether the memo in the rule is to replace the bank memo or if the bank memo should also be kept.
--
Consider using Auto-Add with caution
Finally, you can choose if you want the rule to both categorize transactions and automatically accept them into the client’s 
QuickBooks account. It’s set to do this by default, but use this feature with caution—and when you are experienced in working 
with bank rules. To switch it off, toggle the Auto-add slider.

If you chose to use the auto-add feature, consider including a condition regarding the expected dollar amount. That way, if the 
transaction expense exceeds the typical dollar amount, the rule won’t be applied—and the transaction will show up in the 
For review tab.

If the auto-add feature is used, there’s no need to review the transactions because the rule will be applied automatically.
If you do not use auto-add, the rule will be applied, but the transaction will remain in the For review section. If the rule was 
correct, you or your client can confirm it with a single click to add the transaction to the accounting records. If it was 
incorrect, you can edit the transaction.
--
Check the Rules list
Once saved, a rule is then added to the Rules list in the Transactions center (Accounting -> Rules in the new version of QB). 
From the Actions column, you can edit, copy, disable/enable, move, and delete existing rules. The order in which the rules 
appear is important. When QuickBooks checks transactions from the bank feed, it applies rules in numerical order as listed. Only 
one rule is applied, so the first rule on the list that applies to the transaction is the one that will apply. Rearrange the 
rule priority by dragging the rules up and down.

You can export rules from one client and import them into another.

A rule shouldn’t have been applied if there is a match in QuickBooks. If you find that a rule has been applied when there was a 
match, this could indicate that the rule is too broad or not specific enough. Review the rule conditions and make adjustments as 
needed to ensure it only applies in the intended scenarios.
--
Connecting bank and credit card accounts decreases the need for manual data entry, which streamlines the accounting process.

Creating bank rules helps ensure consistent categorization of transactions. The more you use them, the more QuickBooks will 
learn from them, and make more accurate suggestions for all transactions.

Customizing the settings of the Bank transactions list means you can tailor how the data is displayed in a way that helps you 
better view the details of a transaction to ensure accuracy and efficient processing, and helps your clients get the most from 
their QuickBooks account.

FREQUENT TASKS

Managing the bank feed

Managing the bank feed is all about looking at the transactions in the For review tab of the bank feed and ensuring they are 
accurately recorded. Until this is done, these transactions won’t appear anywhere else in a client’s QuickBooks account.

When transactions come in via the bank feed, QuickBooks analyzes them and suggests matches to existing transactions already 
entered in a client’s QuickBooks account.

Does a rule apply?
If there’s no match, QuickBooks runs each transaction through the rules in the Rules list to determine if any apply. Note that 
QuickBooks will not apply rules if a potential match is found.

What category has been used before for this customer or vendor?
If the transaction hasn’t already been entered elsewhere—for example, as an expense to a vendor—QuickBooks may suggest 
categories based on previously used categories for the same customer or vendor. You decide whether or not to accept 
each suggestion, and record the transaction accordingly.

Trust but verify
This is the critical part of managing the bank feed: you decide whether or not to accept each suggestion QuickBooks makes. 
Never simply accept a suggestion without confirming that it’s correct.

Real-time accounting
Because QuickBooks enables you and your clients to categorize transactions daily, there’s an ongoing clear picture of how 
they are spending their money throughout the month. This means no waiting until the end of the month—or even the next month—for 
information. This brings real-time accounting into view.

Overview of frequent tasks
There are four key tasks that will help you manage the bank feed, starting with the most straightforward and ending with the 
most complex. 

1. Processing receipts
When a client uploads a receipt as an expense, QuickBooks extracts the information and creates a transaction to be reviewed. 
This sits in the Receipts tab rather than the Bank transactions tab of the Transactions center. From here, you can edit the 
receipt, add it to an account, or match it with an existing transaction.

2. Reviewing rules and pairs
Next, you move into the Bank transactions tab and start to work through all the items listed under For review. If you have 
turned off the auto-add feature, you will need to review the transactions that QuickBooks has applied a bank rule to. As rules 
are generally used for frequent transactions, working through them should be straightforward. Likewise, transactions marked as 
paired are usually straightforward.

3. Reviewing money-out transactions
Money-out transactions tend to be very specific and so relatively easy to verify. That’s why matching and categorizing these is 
up next.

4. Reviewing money-in transactions
Money-in transactions often relate to customers. You will explore the nuances that are important to understand when dealing with 
these types of transactions and look at how using the Undeposited Funds account simplifies this part of managing the bank feed.
--
Processing receipts
Let’s take a deeper look at the first task. A receipt uploaded to QuickBooks will show in the Receipts tab, but it’s not recorded 
anywhere else in a client’s QuickBooks account until it’s been reviewed. It either must be categorized and added as a new 
transaction, or matched with an existing transaction.

QuickBooks will look for existing transactions already in QuickBooks that match with the uploaded receipt to help prevent duplicate 
transactions. Note that transactions in the For review tab in the Transactions area are not included in this search.

If no record is found, you can review the receipt and add it as a new transaction. If a record is found, you can review the receipt and the
transaction to confirm that they match, and if so, you can match the receipt to the transaction. If they don’t match, you can add the
receipt as a new transaction.

If two or more records are found that match the receipt, you can review the receipt and the transactions to confirm which one it 
matches with, and if so, you can match the receipt to the transaction. If it doesn’t match with any of the transactions, you can 
add the receipt as a new transaction.
--
Adding receipts after the fact
Receipts can be uploaded after the fact and matched with existing transactions. They can also be forwarded to an email address 
that automatically uploads the receipts into QuickBooks. It’s a good idea to go back and do this from the start of the 
current year.

Storing the receipts in QuickBooks keeps all the records together and tidy, and removes the need for additional files—electronic 
or paper. Automating the process in this way saves time and reduces manual entry errors, and it means the audit process is 
simplified and robust.
--
Reviewing transactions with rules applied
Let’s now move onto the second frequent task, and that’s reviewing transactions that have been identified as fitting a rule or 
paired with another transaction. 

For transactions in the For review tab, QuickBooks first looks to see if the transaction is already in QuickBooks. If so, it 
suggests a match. These matches will be labeled—you’ll look at this in detail later. If there’s no match, QuickBooks runs each 
transaction through the rules in the Rules list to determine if any apply. It only applies one rule per bank transaction, and 
rules are applied in numerical order as they are listed in the Rules tab. The first rule where the transaction meets the 
conditions is the one that QuickBooks applies. You can change the order in which rules are applied by dragging them up and down.

Reviewing paired transactions
If a client moves money between two accounts that are both connected to QuickBooks, QuickBooks identifies both transactions and 
pairs them up. This bank-to-bank transfer matching feature identifies high-confidence matches across connected accounts—for both 
bank and credit card accounts. When transactions for the same date and amount are detected, they show as Paired to another 
transaction. You then decide whether to accept the suggested match or not.

“Paired to another transaction” means that the bank feed item is connected to another bank feed item (usually in a different 
account).” Pairing is typically a transfer between two connected accounts:
	One side = money out
	Other side = money in
Do nothing or review to confirm it’s actually a transfer.

If paired to a credit card (thereby reducing the credit card balance), QuickBooks suggests recording the transaction as a 
Pay down credit card transaction.
--
“Match found” 👉 QuickBooks is telling you: “I found an existing transaction in your books that likely corresponds to this 
bank feed item.” What it’s matching against:
	An already recorded transaction, like:
		Invoice payment
		Expense you entered manually
		Check
		Transfer already created

If the bank feed item is linked to an existing transaction, no new transaction is created. Instead, the bank feed item is 
matched to the existing transaction, and the details from the bank feed are added to that transaction.
If the bank feed item is not linked to an existing transaction, QuickBooks creates a new transaction based on the bank feed item.
--
Fast mental shortcut
Match found = “Already in the books”  Paired = “Other side of the same money”
Ignoring a Match creates duplicates   Breaking a Pair → turns transfers into fake income/expenses
--
Another frequent task is Matching in QuickBooks for both money-in and money-out transactions in the bank feed with transactions 
in QuickBooks.

When you record a transaction in QuickBooks, such as a bill or an expense, you need to match it with the corresponding payment 
that has cleared the bank. If QuickBooks recognizes that a payment in the bank feed has the same characteristics (such as the 
amount or payee) as a transaction already entered in QuickBooks, it suggests a match. You need to verify that the match is 
correct before accepting it.

QuickBooks suggests two types of matches: Exact match and a Linked match. An Exact match means that the bank feed item and the 
existing transaction in QuickBooks have the same amount, date, and payee. A Linked match means that the bank feed item and the 
existing transaction in QuickBooks have the same amount and date, but the payee is different or missing.
When you review a suggested match, you can confirm that it’s correct and accept it, or if it’s not correct, you can reject it 
and either edit the existing transaction in QuickBooks or add the bank feed item as a new transaction.

Linked Match example:
A client enters an expense in QuickBooks for $100 with a payee of “Office Supplies.” When the bank feed pulls in a transaction 
for $100 with a description of “Staples,” QuickBooks may suggest a Linked match because the amount and date match, but the payee 
is different. You would review the details and decide whether to accept the match or not.

Another example of a Linked match:
Your client receives a bill for $750. The client pays the bill but does not record the bill payment in QuickBooks. When the bank 
feed pulls in a transaction for $750 with a description of the vendor, QuickBooks suggests a match with the bill. Accepting the 
match creates a new bill payment transaction, and matches the bank transaction to the bill payment. The original transaction, 
the bill, isn’t replaced. Instead it’s linked to the new transaction, the bill payment—and so this is a linked match.

If the bill payment was recorded in QuickBooks and it was that transaction that was matched to the payment in the bank feed, 
it would be an exact match, because they are both records of the same transaction.

Linked matching also applies to invoices, and it works largely the same as it does for bills (remember, an invoice is the 
equivalent money-in transaction to a bill).

Sales receipts work a little bit differently. If you create a sales receipt and choose Undeposited Funds or Payments to Deposit 
as the deposit account, QuickBooks may suggest a match against the sales receipt. Accepting the match creates a new Bank Deposit 
transaction that’s matched to the bank transaction and linked to the sales receipt, indicating the sales receipt’s funds were 
deposited.

Matching money-out transactions
Let’s now look at the money-out transactions in the For Review list with suggested matches. Expenses are often more straight
forward to process than deposits from customers, because money out is often similar transactions each time.

Categorizing money-out transactions
Often, there’s no existing record in QuickBooks—and therefore no match for a transaction—when it comes in from the bank feed. 
You can categorize and record these transactions directly from the For Review list in the Bank transactions tab.

Once this has been done, the transaction moves to the Categorized tab, an accounting transaction is recorded, and the 
transaction is listed in the categorization history for the same customer or vendor.

Record as transfer or credit card payment
Along with options for categorizing or matching transactions, you can also record transactions as a transfer to another account 
or record them as a credit card payment. Selecting Record as transfer allows you to record a transfer between two accounts in 
your client’s chart of accounts. This is useful for keeping track of funds moving between accounts without affecting income or 
expenses. Selecting Record as credit card payment allows you to record a payment made towards a credit card account, which helps
keep track of credit card balances and payments.

Use Record as transfer to move money between different accounts in your client’s chart of accounts. It ensures that the transfer 
is recorded in both accounts involved, preventing any duplication and keeping records accurate. It transfers the entire amount
—no splits are allowed. This transfer feature is available on money-out and money-in transactions.

Record as transfer is not used for:
	Recording income or expense, so there are no fields for customers, vendors, employees, or products and services items
	Making transfers between a client’s bank account and their vendor’s bank account
--
For bills paid through QuickBooks Bill Pay, when the payment clears the bank, it will be automatically matched to the payment in 
QuickBooks, meaning it won’t appear in the Bank transactions list for manual processing—saving you time. Note, this feature only 
matches bank withdrawals with transactions processed through QuickBooks Bill Pay, and not all transactions are eligible.
--
Using the Undeposited Funds account
Money-in payments generally relate to sales made to customers, and they often aren’t deposited into your client’s bank account 
right away. Instead these payments are grouped together and deposited at the bank as a single deposit. This means that individual 
payments received from customers won’t show up in the bank feed. Instead the total deposit amount will show. It can be confusing, 
and if handled incorrectly, it could create an accounting issue and a customer service issue, so it’s important to get it right.

Accurately matching money-in transactions in the bank feed to transactions already recorded in QuickBooks is underpinned by 
using the Undeposited Funds account workflow. Keep in mind that parts of this process are about what happens in real life 
(outside of QuickBooks) and parts are about what happens inside QuickBooks!
--
Categorizing money-in transactions
Categorize money-in transactions with caution! You should usually only need to do this for money-in that doesn’t involve a 
customer, such as an IRS reimbursement.

Categorizing for different clients
1) Clients using the Sales center
If a client is using the Sales center in QuickBooks and the transaction involves a customer, there should be no need to categorize via the banking area. It should have been done earlier in the workflow when recording the money-in transaction as either payment received for an invoice or as a sales receipt—customers should be selected from the Customer list and sales should be categorized using Items from the Products and services list. 

How to categorize in the bank feed
However, if this hasn’t happened, you can categorize in the Transactions tab:

From the Type of transaction dropdown, select Sales receipt if a sale was made to a customer—otherwise, select Deposit.
Sales receipts can then be categorized using the Product/Service dropdown.
Deposits can be categorized using the Account dropdown to allocate to the appropriate account in the chart of accounts.

2) Clients not using the Sales center
Sometimes clients won’t use the Sales center in QuickBooks; instead they use a third-party app. 

Examples include medical businesses that have strict customer privacy regulations so require additional functionality when 
dealing with customer records, and e-commerce businesses that handle all sales via their online shop. 
These clients may record directly to income, without ever touching the Sales center in QuickBooks.

How to categorize in the bank feed
It may be appropriate to use a rule to categorize this type of income.
--
Some tips for matching money-out and money-in transactions.
Here are some things to look out for when reviewing and recording transactions:

Look for old checks
When looking for potential matches, QuickBooks looks at transactions over a 90-day time frame. If a check is very old, QuickBooks 
won’t necessarily find it. So, when you select Find match, you would need to expand the date range and then you should find the 
old check. Otherwise if no match is found, it’s safe to assume it is not in QuickBooks, and so it could be recorded as an expense 
again.

Double-check the account
Expenses can get entered twice if they go to the wrong account, for example, being allocated to a credit card instead of a debit 
card. So always do an extra check to be sure that expenses are allocated to the correct account.

Double-check the amount
Amounts don’t always neatly line up. For example, a bill from the electricity company gets entered into QuickBooks as being $100. 
But when it comes in via the bank feed, the actual amount taken is $130. The match gets missed and the bill gets entered again.

If not sure where the funds are coming from, ask the client if they have any additional documentation or information about the 
transaction that could help clarify the discrepancy. This could include receipts, invoices, or notes about the transaction.

Make sure the Undeposited Funds account is being used correctly
Here’s what can go wrong when the Undeposited Funds account isn’t used…
A client receives three payments of $50 and records them in QuickBooks, allocating directly to their bank account instead of the 
Undeposited Funds account. They deposit the three payments in a single deposit at the bank for $150.

When the $150 deposit comes into QuickBooks in the bank feed, you may not connect the three $50 payments, and instead record $150 
to income. This means that the income is overstated and the customer accounts continue to reflect open invoices.
--
When matching transactions between the bank feed and QuickBooks, keep in mind that while dates are a helpful clue, transactions 
can sometimes take a while to post, so they won’t always necessarily line up. Use your judgment and trust but verify!

OCCASIONAL TASKS
Explore the tasks likely to be done on a monthly basis for banking in Intuit QuickBooks.

1. Reconcile bank and credit card accounts
2. Review and categorize bank feed transactions
3. Ensure all income and expenses are recorded
4. Review and resolve any discrepancies
5. Generate and review financial reports

A couple of days after the statement is issued by the bank is a great time to do the reconciliation, because it gives everything 
time to clear the bank. Compare the list of transactions on the statement with what’s entered in QuickBooks. Connecting accounts 
to QuickBooks and using the Reconcile feature streamlines the process, and for most clients reconciliation will be a breeze. 
Others may need a little more work!

When you’re done reconciling, the difference between the amount in the QuickBooks accounts and the bank statements should be 
$0.00. But let’s be clear—while that $0 difference is definitely a goal, it’s not the only goal. Ensuring that you understand 
what hasn't cleared the bank and why, and whether you need to take action with those transactions, is also the goal. Put your 
detective hat on. Investigate all transactions for accuracy. That zero only means that everything is balanced when it’s been 
investigated and verified.

Overview of occasional tasks
There are three key tasks to reconciling a bank or credit card account. 
1. View the Bank or Credit Card account statement
2. Reconcile the Bank or Credit Card account
3. Resolve any issues
--
Manually uploading statements
If the client’s bank doesn’t automatically upload bank statements to QuickBooks, you should upload them for recordkeeping 
purposes at the end of reconciling when prompted, or by selecting the History by account option from the Reconcile screen.
It’s best practice to do this because it creates a robust ‘paper trail’ for review and audit purposes.
---
If an account is riddled with adjustments (as shown on the History by account) then you may want to undo the reconciliation. This 
allows you to start over so that you can find the discrepancies more easily.

You might also want to undo if the reconciliation was forced, which means it was saved even though the difference wasn’t zero. 
You can identify a forced reconciliation because QuickBooks will create a Reconciliation Adjustment entry shown in the 
History by Account column, and the entry is on the Profit and Loss under the Reconciliation Discrepancies account.

You might also want to undo if the reconciliation was forced, which means it was saved even though the difference wasn’t zero. 
You can identify a forced reconciliation because QuickBooks will create a Reconciliation Adjustment entry shown in the History 
by Account column, and the entry is on the Profit and Loss under the Reconciliation Discrepancies account.

Only an accountant user (you) can undo a reconciliation. To undo a reconciliation, go to History by account in the Reconcile 
screen. It’s a good idea to first download the reconciliation as a PDF. You can do this from the Action column—you can also 
undo from here.

To undo or not to undo? Be very cautious of undoing for a closed period or when taxes have been done. Instead, some accounting 
pros prefer to make adjustments via a journal entry so that they can provide a more detailed explanation for the adjustment, 
especially for a clean up. Others allow a reconciliation adjustment to remain, if the amount is immaterial.
---
Managing the bank feed frequently and thoughtfully throughout the month makes end-of-month reconciliation much more 
straightforward. When you carefully match and categorize transactions in the bank feed, fewer issues will need to be fixed during 
reconciliation.
---
You can use the Reconciliation Discrepancy Report as part of correcting beginning balances. That Reconciliation Discrepancy 
Report report is only available when the beginning balance is incorrect via the "We can help you fix it" link from the 
discrepancy alert message; it’s not available via the Reports center. 
---
The Reconciliation report can be accessed from the Reconcile tab via the Summary link, and it’s accessible via the Reports center.

The Reconciliation summary lists all the reconciliations done for all the bank and credit card accounts in the client’s chart 
of accounts. The Reconciliation summary also available for other balance sheet accounts that are reconciled. QuickBooks Online 
Certification Level 2 discusses how this reconciliation process can be performed on nonbank/credit card accounts.
---
You can view a Reconciliation report for every reconciliation done for a client. There’s an option to Save as PDF, which is best 
practice to do for general documentation and if, for example, your firm needs to save workpapers outside of QuickBooks as proof 
of work done.

The Reconciliation report starts by showing any changes that have happened to the reconciled transactions covered in the report 
since the reconciliation, if there are any. As a general rule, if there are changees you will likely need to find out more about 
why these changes have been made. It may be helpful to refer to the Audit Log to dig in. Access it from the General settings 
gear, at the top right of the screen. It lists any changes, so it’s a good place to start your investigation.

The Summary section of the report shows a summary, first of the bank statement information and then the register details—that is, 
what is inside QuickBooks.

The Details section of the report lists all the items that have cleared the bank. You can select each item in this list to 
view them.

The report ends with Additional Information. It shows what hasn’t cleared the bank in the following sections:
Uncleared checks and payments as of the date of the reconciliation.
Uncleared checks and payments after the date of the reconciliation.
Uncleared deposits and other credits as of the date of the reconciliation.
Uncleared deposits and other credits after the date of the reconciliation.

Those items marked as of the date of the reconciliation are factored into the summary details above and so need to be investigated.
Those listed after the date of the reconciliation don’t need to be investigated because they will form part of the next 
reconciliation.
---
Once you’ve looked at the Reconciliation report, it can be valuable to go to the Client overview dashboard, accessed via the 
left-hand menu, and check the Banking activity. It will show a summary of all bank and credit card accounts and their 
reconciliation status, as well as non-bank accounts that have been selected to list there as well.
---
The two key reports are the Reconciliation Discrepancy Report, which appears when you need to fix a beginning balance, and the 
Reconciliation report, which is generated when a reconciliation is done. Review both to perform checks and balances to see where 
your client’s business is.

Because it comes at the end of the banking workflow, the Reconciliation report can be a rich source to help you identify issues 
earlier on in the client’s workflow, as well as providing details about the reconciliation of the bank account to the activity 
in QuickBooks.

Light-touch advisory opportunities in the banking workflow range from simply walking a client through the setup process, to 
deeper insights based on how you interpret the data in the Reconciliation report into their day-to-day workflow and processes.

Books review process
While it’s not part of the Book review process, when reviewing the transactions at period end, it can also be a good idea to 
review any transactions that have been voided or deleted. 

To review any transactions that have been voided or deleted, go to Accountant tools and select Voided/deleted transactions. This 
will take you to a filtered view of the audit log, showing any transactions that have been voided or deleted.

In Final review, QuickBooks pulls together a list of all the accounts payable and receivable that are older than 90 days. You 
will also see links to the financial reports. These, and any other reports you have added to Final Review, should be reviewed to 
verify that the information in them is correct. 

The first task in Final review is to investigate any accounts payable and receivable that are older than 90 days. Accounts 
receivable transactions should be investigated for possible duplicates, and accounts payable should be looked at in case they 
still need to be paid but cashflow is an issue, or if they have been paid but it’s not been properly recorded in QuickBooks.

To prove the accounts receivable amounts in the balance sheet, run an Accounts Receivable Aging Summary Report and confirm the 
figures match. 

If the Accounts Receivable Aging Summary Report shows credit balances, check to see why. It could be that the client owes their 
customer money, or the customer has paid for a product or service but hasn’t been invoiced.

If there are any invoices that need chasing down you can change the date range on the A/R Aging Summary Report to see if any 
invoices have been paid since period end to see if chasing down the invoices is necessary.

If there is anything in the report that has remained unpaid for a long time, it might be a good idea to discuss with the client 
how to handle it. If it’s considered that the amount will never be paid, leaving it in accounts receivable will mean the client’s 
books aren’t an accurate reflection of their business.

Standard Item Types
The first step in adding a new item to the products and services list is to select the appropriate item type. Here’s a summary

Recording noncustomer deposits
Your client may receive a deposit that isn’t related to a customer transaction, such as vendor or tax agency refunds, cash 
contributions from owners, or proceeds from loans.
Although they’re not directly part of the sales and customers workflow, they’re included here so you can record them accurately, 
combine them with customer payments into a deposit, and code them to the right account—and not confuse them with sales income.

It is critical that every transaction recorded for a customer involves one of the sales forms—estimate, invoice, sales receipt, 
credit memo, or refund receipt.

item codes.

When an estimate is created in QuickBooks, you can track the transaction’s progress. 
Estimates are nonaccounting transactions (sometimes called nonposting transactions). This means they aren’t recorded in any 
general ledger accounts, and don't affect account balances. As a result, they won’t appear in financial statements like the 
profit and loss or balance sheet. They do appear in the details for the customer that they apply to.

Clients who have created at least one estimate, will see the Estimates tab in the Sales center, which is where you can create, 
view, and manage customer estimates. Otherwise, begin at the +New butto to create the first estimate.

eliminates human error. Once it becomes an invoice, QuickBooks automatically marks the estimate as Converted and Closed in the 
Sales center, even if only partial quantities are added to the invoice.

When filling out an invoice you can upload an existing file such as a PDF, an image of handwritten notes or a text file.

When a client creates an invoice, it is auto recorded as income and an entry is entered into the accounts receivable account. 
When the client receives payment, they close the receivable and record the money as deposited into the bank.

Clients usually issue a sales receipt when customers pay for a product or service at the time of the sale. When a client creates 
a sales receipt in QuickBooks, it increases income and makes an entry in a bank account because payment is already included in 
the transaction.

Note that unlike invoices, when recording a sale via a sales receipt, the sale and payment are recorded at the same time, so 
there’s no need to receive payment in the same way as with invoices, making the process more efficient.

In QuickBooks Online, when you use a Sales Receipt, the system records both sides of the transaction automatically, but in 
different places in the books. Here’s exactly where each part goes:

1. The Sale
Recorded in an Income (Revenue) account
Shows up on your Profit & Loss (P&L) report
This is what increases your revenue
2. The Payment
Recorded in a Cash-type account, such as:
	Bank account (Checking) or Undeposited Funds (if you haven’t grouped deposits yet)
It then shows up on your Balance Sheet and increase your cash.

A Sales Receipt skips Accounts Receivable entirely, versus an Invoice that expects a later payment → uses A/R.
Sales Receipt → immediate payment → goes straight to Income and to Cash.

So for a Sales Receipt, QuickBooks is doing two things at once:
	Crediting Income (the sale)
	Debiting Cash/Bank (the payment)

Using the Undeposited Funds account process:

1) Record the sale in QuickBooks
Clients use invoices or sales receipts to record sales transactions in QuickBooks.

2) Collect the payment

Undeposited Funds account. This temporary holding account tracks payments your client receives from customers that aren’t yet

	Decreases (credits) the Accounts Receivable account

Recording that you’ve received payment via a sales receipt:
	Increases (debits) the Undeposited Funds account
	Increases (credits) the Sales account

4) Make the deposit at the bank
Your client deposits the payments at their bank and it’s likely that they'll fill out a deposit slip, stating the total amount 
deposited.

5) Record the bank deposit in QuickBooks
When your client deposits the payments at the bank, this should be recorded in QuickBooks.
Holding payments received in the Undeposited Funds account until they’re deposited lets you record a single Bank deposit in 
QuickBooks for these multiple payments. This makes it easier to match the deposit to the transaction that clears the bank 
and comes into QuickBooks via the bank feed.

When the bank deposit is recorded in QuickBooks, it:
	Increases (debits) the bank account
	Decreases (credits) the Undeposited Funds account

6) Deposit transaction comes in via bank feed
When a client’s bank account is connected to QuickBooks, bank transactions come in via the bank feed, ready to match them to 
existing records in QuickBooks, or to categorize them if there’s no record.

Because the payments have been grouped together and deposited at the bank as a single deposit, the bank feed shows the total 
deposit amount.

Match bank feed transaction with deposit record
When the bank feed transaction is matched with the bank deposit recorded in QuickBooks, the register updates accordingly ready 
for month-end reconciliation.
--
Record payments you haven’t deposited at your bank in your Undeposited Funds account. Once you’ve deposited at least two or more 
payments, you can record them as a single deposit to another account.
You only need to use the Undeposited Funds account account if:
* You’re depositing more than one payment at a time at your bank. If not, you can record the payment directly in another account.
* You haven’t connected your bank and credit card accounts to QuickBooks, so QuickBooks doesn’t have your bank info.
* If your invoice payments aren’t being processed with QuickBooks Payments. 

QuickBooks takes care of invoice payments processed with QuickBooks Payments for you. No need to put these in Undeposited Funds.
Best practice is to always use Undeposited Funds.

When clients use QuickBooks Payments, customer deposits are a breeze—essentially payment and accounting happen at the same time, 
eliminating manual entries.

Clients can add links to invoices so customers can pay online with PayPal, Venmo, credit card, or ACH bank transfer—just by 
following the link in the email. Payments can also be processed in person or over the phone. Once a payment is made, everything 
is entered inside QuickBooks automatically:
	Payment is recorded in QuickBooks
	Deposited into the bank account and automatically matched to the deposit when it clears the bank
	Transaction fees are recorded and properly categorized

Recording noncustomer deposits
Let’s look at the final task in this workflow. Clients sometimes receive deposits that aren’t related to customer transactions, 
such as vendor or tax agency refunds, cash contributions from owners, or proceeds from loans. These need to be recorded 
accurately so that they’re not confused with sales income, which shows on sales reports.

Because these deposit transactions aren’t income and don’t relate to customers, don’t use sales forms to record them. Use the 
Bank deposit feature to record and categorize any money coming into the business that isn’t related to sales.

Use the Bank deposits feature when:
* Your client receives funds from loan proceeds or contributions of cash from owners.
* Your client receives a refund from a vendor and wants to record it directly against the associated expense account. 
* Your client receives a refund from a tax agency and needs to record it to reduce the tax liability or expense account.
* An employee reimburses your client’s company, and your client needs to record the reimbursement against the employee loan 
  receivable account.
* Your client doesn’t record income using sales transactions (invoices or sales receipts) and wants to record deposits directly 
  to income accounts. Generally this isn’t best practice, but there are exceptions—for example, a medical practice that uses 
  medical billing software to keep track of their accounts receivable to stay compliant with privacy legislation, or an 
  e-commerce store that doesn’t connect to QuickBooks.

Generally you shouldn’t use Bank deposits to record sales as it doesn’t track individual sales. Doing so will lead to a lack 
of meaningful information on sales reports; the client won’t be able to send their customers documentation, such as invoices or 
sales receipts; and the transaction won’t show up in the customer’s activity log.

Every transaction recorded for a customer should involve one of the sales forms. This ensures that customer records and sales 
data are recorded accurately.

Invoices are used to request payment from customers for products or services provided on credit, and sales receipts are used to 
record sales and payments received at the time of the transaction.

The Undeposited Funds account is a temporary holding account that tracks payments received from customers that have not been 
deposited to your client’s bank account. When the payments are deposited at the bank, this should be recorded in QuickBooks.

OCCASIONAL TASKS
Explore the tasks likely to be done on a monthly basis for sales and customers in Intuit QuickBooks.

There are three key occasional tasks for sales and customers. Get an overview of each one here before diving into the details.

1. Issuing customer statements
A customer statement is a summary of a customer's account activity over a specific period of time. The customer statement 
includes details such as invoices, payments, credits, and outstanding balances, allowing customers to review their transactions 
and current account status. They are a valuable communication tool between a business and their customers, helping customers 
stay informed about their financial obligations and facilitating timely payments.

2. Issuing credit memos and customer refunds
Clients may encounter situations where they complete a project sooner than expected, purchase more materials than they used, or 
perhaps a customer has to return a faulty item. In these cases, the client may need to either refund all or part of a payment to 
the customer, or issue them with a credit memo to use against a future sale.

3. Using recurring sales transactions
Recurring transactions makes it easy to set up regular transactions for automatic processing as templates. Recurring transactions 
can be used even as a reminder to process a transaction. The most common transactions for customers are recurring invoices and 
recurring sales receipts.
--
Issuing customer statements
Clients who issue invoices may find it helpful to send customer statements on a regular basis—such as monthly or quarterly—to 
keep their customers informed about their account activity. These statements can serve as gentle reminders about upcoming payment 
deadlines and help with collections. They also provide a summary of the transaction history of the customer.

There are three types of customer statements in QuickBooks.

1) The Balance Forward statement shows a beginning balance, together with a list of sales transactions (including invoices, 
payments, and credit memos) for a selected date range, plus a running balance.

The Balance Forward statement is perfect for situations where a client wants to show activity during a specified time period, 
together with a running balance of the total owed on their account.

2) With the Open Item statement, it’s possible to produce a list of all open unpaid invoices from the last 365 days.
This statement type is great for clients who want to provide details for the year of all unpaid invoices and open credit 
memos—and possibly unapplied payments—to their customers.

3) Use the Transaction Statement to show all sales receipts and invoices—and the total amount of payments applied to each 
invoice—for a specified time period. Clients might use the Transaction Statement when there is a large volume of transactions 
for a customer so they can see how their payments have been applied.
--
*A random note about invoices:
When creating an invoice, there’s a Message on statement field where you can add memo text that will appear on statements 
alongside the invoice number. This is a great place to add a note about the invoice, such as "Payment for landscaping services 
in June." This can help customers understand what the invoice is for when they see it on their statement, which can encourage 
timely payment and reduce confusion.
--
When a client needs to issue a refund to a customer, a client can do so by creating a Credit Memo or a Refund Receipt. A Credit 
Memo is typically used to document a reduction in the amount owed by a customer or to add a credit to a future invoice. A Refund 
Receipt is used to process a cash refund. Both options allow clients to effectively manage customer refunds and maintain accurate 
financial records.

If your client does not use invoices, the client can still issue  Credit Memos to their customers.

Credit Memos are accounting transactions that can be applied to a customer’s invoice as part or full payment.
Credit Memos do not refund the customer, so they are not a money-out transaction.
Credit Memos reduce Accounts Receivable, but does not reduce Cash.

Use Credit Memos when a client wants to provide a customer with a credit to be applied to an open or future invoice, not when 
they want to issue an immediate refund.

Think of a refund receipt as a reverse sales receipt. A sales receipt increases revenue and increases cash, a refund receipt 
decreases revenue and decreases cash. Refund receipts can be used by all clients.

Refund Receipts:
Are money-out accounting transactions.
Reduce the specified payment account and cash.
Don’t affect Accounts Receivable.

Use Refund Receipts when:
A customer paid an invoice but decides to return the item or has decided that they don't want the service.

Best practice for credit memos
1) Why use a Credit Memo instead of just editing the transaction?
When a client needs to issue a refund to a customer, best practice is to create a credit memo first, and then apply it to the
customer’s next invoice. This way, the credit is applied to the customer’s account and reduces the amount they owe on their 
next invoice. This is better than issuing a refund receipt because it keeps the credit on the customer’s account, which can be 
helpful for future transactions. It also keeps the customer’s transaction history more accurate and complete, which can be 
helpful for reporting and tracking purposes. This is especially true when the original invoice is in a prior period, because 
making a change would cause the accounts in QuickBooks to become out of balance with the tax return and change previously 
reported amounts.

2) Why not just charge less on the next invoice?
Charging less on the next invoice doesn’t give an accurate trail of what’s actually happened. Creating a new transaction in 
QuickBooks for each exchange between the client and the customer is important. It gives clarity to the timing and sequence of 
events with a customer. 

Just charging less on the next invoice can also affect the timing and balances of sales tax liabilities and inventory movement 
in and out of the company.

3) What if a Credit Memo has been issued but the customer wants a refund instead of a credit?
Void the credit memo and create a refund.

Remember it was mentioned earlier that Products and Services items don’t have to be things that your client specifically sells?
Credit Memos aren’t just for reimbursing clients, they can be issued at any time. For example, as part of a loyalty reward or 
referral incentive program—or as a goodwill gesture. 

If a client wants to set up a referral program, they can add a new Service item called "Thank You For Referring Us" that’s mapped 
to a Discounts Income account. They can then use this new service item when they create the Credit Memo.

When Automatically Apply Credits is configured to be on, QuickBooks automatically applies credits to the oldest open invoices 
for the same customer, or if there are not any old invoices, apply the credit to the next invoice created for the same customer. 
Most companies turn on the Automatically Apply Credits setting.
--
The final occasional task is about Recurring Transactions. Recurring Transactions can be created in many workflows including 
sales receipts, invoices, bills, checks, deposits and expenses. In relation to sales and customers, Recurring Transactions are a 
great way to automatically invoice customers for fixed fee billing for a service contract, such as accounting services and IT 
services. The contract may be billed each month for 12 months, and so the sales receipts can be set up to process automatically 
on the 1st of every month.

A Recurring Transaction can be set up to occur on a scheduled or unscheduled basis, or as a reminder to send a transaction. Once 
the parameters for the scheduled Recurring Transaction have been set up, QuickBooks will automate the process. An existing 
Recurring Transaction can be used as a template for creating other Recurring Transactions.

Recurring Transactions can be set up using three template types: scheduled, unscheduled, and reminder. You can access them from 
the General settings gear at the top right of the screen.

Note that an unscheduled transaction is simply a transaction that was created manually and is not tied to any recurring schedule.

Use a Scheduled template to automatically create a Recurring Transaction at a set frequency or on set dates.

For example, your client could automatically invoice customers for regular services provided at fixed prices, such as weekly 
gardening services. 

Recurring transactions and QuickBooks Payments
For clients with QuickBooks Payments enabled, there are a couple of ways Recurring transactions and QuickBooks Payments can be 
incorporated into a recurring model.

1) Set up recurring sales Transactions
Recurring invoices and sales receipts can have QuickBooks Payments enabled. Here are a couple of use cases.
Case 1: With Autopay enabled, customers make payments for recurring invoices automatically. Customers can sign up and enter 
their payment info through the invoice once, and their payments will process automatically for future recurring invoices.

Case 2: A client creates a recurring scheduled sales receipt for a customer and selects the option that gives the client 
permission to process the payment. The credit card or ACH info for the customer will already have been entered, so the Sales 
Receipt serves two purposes:
	The Sales Receipt charges the customer’s bank or credit card account.
	The client sends the receipt to the customer automatically.

2) Set up a recurring payment
A recurring payment can be selected from the +New button for clients that have QuickBooks Payments enabled. Recurring payments 
automatically charge your customers on a scheduled basis (daily, weekly, monthly, or annually). One-time fees, like initial setup 
fees, can be added to the first charge. You select which payment methods to accept, including cards or ACH bank transfers. When 
you create a recurring payment, your customer receives an email from QuickBooks to review the details. They enter their preferred 
payment information and accept the recurring payment. They’re automatically charged on schedule every period and sent a receipt 
for the payment by email.

Keep an eye on recurring transactions
It’s a good idea for your clients to regularly review their recurring transactions to avoid sending automated invoices for 
canceled or delayed work.

It’s important to note that a customer, vendor, a product or service, and an account can not be made inactive if it is being used 
on a recurring transaction.

Info from practice questions
A credit memo is the reverse of an invoice. An invoice acknowledges that a customer owes your client money, a credit memo 
acknowledges that a customer has a credit on their account. Credit memos are accounting transactions that can be applied to a

A refund receipt is the opposite of a sales Receipt. A sales receipt increases revenue and increases cash, a refund receipt

Reports and Insights

Explore the key reports that bring together data entered in the sales and customers process and consider the light-touch 
advisory opportunities available in this workflow.

The reports associated with sales center data sit in two sections of the Reports center(actually they are located under Standard 
Reports): The two sections are "Who owes you" and "Sales and Customers". The section "Who owes you" provides information about 
accounts receivable: this is who owes your client money through invoices to customers. The section "Sales and Customers"" digs 
in and looks at revenue generated by customer and by product.

The difference between sales reports and income reports
Sales reports and income reports serve different purposes and provide distinct types of financial information. A sales report 
focuses on the revenue generated from sales activities. It provides details on the sales transactions within a specified period. 

An Income Report—also known as an income statement or profit and loss statement—provides a broader view of a business’s 
financial performance over a period, showing not just sales but also other income and expenses.

Accounting pros tend to use the profit and loss statement as the primary source of reporting on income. But when a client is 
using QuickBooks for their sales, they may need more information than just a total number. They may need details on specific 
products or services sold.

By analyzing both income reports and sales reports, businesses can gain detailed insights into their sales performance and 
overall financial status, supporting better financial management and strategic planning.
--
Some key reports
Keep in mind that summary reports provide overview data, such as a simple statement of gross profit. Detailed reports show a 
breakdown of transactions in a particular period. List reports show detailed information about list items, such as customers 
or products and services. It’s up to you to determine which reports are the best for a client’s particular needs.

1) Accounts Receivable Aging Summary and Accounts Receivable Aging Detail reports show all customers who currently owe the 
client and how old their balances are. Accounts Receivable Aging Summary and Accounts Receivable Aging Detail reports list all 
open (unpaid) invoices and how long they have been open, regardless of whether they are overdue or not. The Accounts Receivable 
Aging Summary report presents by customer, and the Accounts Receivable Aging Detail report presents by aging—grouping outstanding 
amounts by how old they are.

To check for accuracy, you could run these reports and compare the results to the balance sheet (run using accrual basis) with 
the same "as of" date as the accounts receivable aging report. The accounts receivable line on the balance sheet should be the 
same as the total on the Accounts Receivable Aging Summary and the Accounts Receivable Aging Detail reports.

Use these reporst when a client wants to prioritize which customers to contact to get outstanding payments from.

2) Customer Balance Summary and Customer Balance Detail reports contain similar data to the Accounts Receivable Aging reports, 
but without the aging details. Use Customer Balance Summary and Customer Balance Detail reports when a client wants to get an 
overall general view of the open accounts receivable balances for each customer. If they want to look at a granular view where 
you see all of the dates of transactions, then you would use the Customer Balance Detail report.

3) The Collections Report has the same data as on the Customer Balance Detail report, with the addition of customer phone 
numbers. You can customize the Collections Report to show other useful details, such as email addresses. You can also choose 
how many days past the due date so that the report only displays overdue invoices. Use when a client wants to Collect the 
outstanding amounts.

4) Customer Contact List report contains all the contact details held for each customer. Note that when a customer is 
displayed with a colon after their name and then additional details, this indicates that they are either a sub-customer or that 
it’s a project for a customer. Use the Customer Contact List report when a client wants to be sure their customer database is up 
to date. It’s a good idea to highlight this report to your client and recommend that they take a look at it every so often and 
clean it up by merging customers or making some inactive.

5) Sales by Customer Summary and Detail reports show the client’s customers and how much income they generated in the defined 
period. Top customers can be viewed by sorting the Customer summary report by total in descending order. The Customer Detail 
report gives more information about a particular customer’s buying trends. Use when a client wants to Identify customers with 
the same buying trends.

6) Income by Customer Summary report contains similar data as the Sales by Customer Summary and Detail reports, with the 
addition of expenses per customer, as long as they have enabled tracking expenses by customer.

The Customer Summary report provides a summarized view of the income and expenses by customer, whereas the Profit and Loss By 
Customer gives more detail on the income and expenses by account. Use when a client wants to get a great overview of performance 
by customer.

7) Sales by Product/Service Summary and Sales by Product/Service Detail reports show how many of each item on the products and 
services list were sold in a period, and how much income they generated. They are the inverse of the Sales by Customer Summary 
and Detail reports and should have the same total amount for the same period.

Use the Product/Service Summary and Sales by Product/Service Detail reports when a client wants to see the quantity (this must 
be enabled) and the amount of products and services sold. The Product/Service Summary and Sales by Product/Service Detail reports
can also show the percentage of total sales and the average price. This information can help to determine which are the biggest 
products or services sold and which are the lowest.
--
Cash versus accrual reporting
Most QuickBooks reports are available for either cash or accrual accounting methods. With cash-basis accounting, income and 
expenses are recorded when payment is received. With accrual-basis accounting, income is recorded when the revenue is earned, 
regardless of payment date, and expenses are recorded when the expense is incurred, regardless of payment date.

By nature, accounts receivable is accrual-based accounting. Using accrual-based accounting, you record income when earned and 
get paid later, so there is no option for the accounting method on those reports. For sales reports however, there is an option 
for the accounting method, and so the data and totals included in these reports will be different depending on the accounting 
method chosen when running a report.

Note: Remember that expenses are incurred. Revenue is earned.
--
Some Key Notes:

Use sales and income reports to get the whole picture
Sales reports focus on revenue from sales activities, while income reports offer a broader view of financial performance over 
a period, including sales, income, and expenses. Analyzing both helps businesses gain insights into sales and overall financial 
status, supporting better decision-making.

Run the right report at the right time
The reports associated with sales center data sit in two sections of the Reports center. Those under Who owes you provides 
information about accounts receivable: who owes your client money through invoices to customers. Reports under Sales and customers 
look at revenue generated by customer and by product.

Look for advisory opportunities
The key light-touch advisory opportunity in the sales and customers workflow is helping clients to embed best practices for 
consistently recording their sales transactions in QuickBooks, so that revenue is accurately stated.

Adjusting options in the account and settings
Access this area from the General settings gear, top right of the screen. It’s the first option in the Your company column.
Let’s start with the Sales tab on the left, and look at the section covering Sales form content. This part dictates what appears by default on all sales forms. 

Preferred invoice terms sets the default payment date. Net 30 means payment is due 30 days from the date on the invoice.

Preferred delivery method default determines how sales forms are delivered to newly created customers. The options are to Print 
later, Send later, or None. Choosing to print or send later means you can batch them and review them altogether. This can help 
to reduce errors and streamline workflows.

You can change the default delivery method for a customer by editing the customer on the customer list. You can also change the delivery method when you create individual invoices or other sales forms.

Shipping adds shipping-related fields to sales forms: date, tracking number, destination, subtotal to sales forms. 

If you want Custom fields to appear as default on sales forms, go to Settings, then Lists, and then Custom fields.

Custom transaction numbers lets you view and change the sales transaction numbers, rather than allowing QuickBooks to automatically assign numbers.

Enabling Service date adds a field if you need to track the date a service was performed separately from the invoice date.

Enabling Discount adds a field to invoices and other sales forms to enable discounts to be added.

Enabling Deposit adds a field to invoices so you can subtract a customer deposit from the total to calculate the balance due.

If your client wants to Accept tips from their customers on sales receipts and invoices, they can enable it here. This is a default setting that will apply to all future invoices.

Financing
With the Customer financing option enabled, when a client sends an estimate of more than $1,000, their customer will see a financing option powered by Intuit Credit Karma. Financing options are only shown to individuals, not to business customers, but we’re working to bring business financing options into QuickBooks.
There’s no cost to your client. Their customer pays a competitive annual percentage rate (APR) to the lender they select. This is based on credit approval via Credit Karma.
Note that this setting is at the company level, so it can’t be controlled on a transaction-by-transaction basis.

Products and services section
Next up, the Products and services section.

Here you can choose to Show Product/Service column on sales forms so you can choose from a list of products and services to 
include. Turning off this option will also turn off:
Show items table on expense and purchase forms
Use purchase orders
Track inventory quantity on hand
You can also choose to Show SKU column. A SKU is a stock keeping unit, also called an item number. It’s unique and has up to 100 alphanumeric characters that you can assign to a product. You can use the SKU to quickly search for or identify an item.
Enabling price rules means your client can provide discounts for specific products to certain customers for a specified amount of time. This is covered in the QuickBooks Online Certification Level 2.

By enabling Track quantity and price/rate you're adding Quantity and Rate fields to sales forms so you can track this information 
for products and services.

Enabling Track inventory quantity on hand is how you turn on inventory tracking for Plus and Advanced plans. QuickBooks always considers the first items a client purchases as the first items they sell. In other words, the costing method for inventory in QuickBooks is First In First Out (FIFO).

Messages Section
The Messages section is where you can adjust the content of default emails that are sent out with invoices, estimates, credit 
memos, sales receipts, statements, and refund receipts. You can modify these emails at the time you send them. Note that you 
can replace these emails for estimates, invoices, and sales receipts by setting up templates when you customize form styles, as you just saw in the previous setup task.
Reminders
QuickBooks can be set up to automatically send reminders to customers when invoices are, or will soon be, overdue. The Reminders section is where you can adjust the content, number, and frequency of reminder emails sent for open invoices. You can see when reminders were sent by going to the Sales center and then All sales. QuickBooks adds Reminded in the Status column for invoices that have been sent a reminder.

email. Note that you can replace these defaults for estimates, invoices, and sales receipts by setting up templates when you 
customize form styles, as you just saw in the previous setup task.
Statements
The final section on the Sales tab relates to the details you can include and adjust on a customer statement, such as the amounts owed and by how many days they’re overdue.
Automation settings
Another tab to check is the Advanced (or global) settings, specifically Automation. Selections made here will affect all transactions, not just those relating to sales.

Do you want to Pre-fill forms with previously entered content? This automatically fills various fields of the form, based on the last saved transaction for that customer, vendor, or employee. 

Do you want to Automatically apply credits to the next invoice you create for the same customer? Most companies turn on this setting, but it’s not appropriate for all. For example, a credit to a customer for a prepayment and your client wants to choose when to apply it.

Do you want to Automatically invoice unbilled activity? Enabling this displays notifications in the Activity feed on the Home page.

Chart_Of_Accounts

A Chart of Accounts (COA) is:
The master list of every account your business uses to organize its financial activity. 
Every transaction you enter must land in one of these accounts.
The number of accounts that can be set up in a client’s QuickBooks account will depend on which plan they are on. 
Each account within the chart of accounts should be set up in the same way, with the same set of details. 

What the Chart of Accounts actually does:
It organizes your business into five main buckets:
	Assets – What you own
	Liabilities – What you owe
	Equity – What belongs to the owner
	Income – Money coming in
	Expenses – Money going out
That’s it. Everything fits somewhere in those five buckets.
---
Simple example: A basic Chart of Accounts might include:
	Assets: Checking Account, Savings Account, Accounts Receivable, Equipment
	Liabilities: Credit Card, Loan Payable, Accounts Payable
	Equity: Owner’s Equity, Retained Earnings
	Income: Service Income, Product Sales
	Expenses: Rent, Utilities, Supplies, Insurance

*Each of those is an “account” in the chart.
---
The Chart of Accounts determines:
	How your Profit & Loss is structured
	How your Balance Sheet looks
	How clean your reporting is
	How easy tax prep will be

*If the COA is messy, everything downstream is messy.
---
What beginners do wrong:
Create too many accounts
Create duplicate accounts
Confuse categories with accounts
Add accounts instead of using subaccounts
Let auto-rules create junk

More accounts ≠ better accounting. Clear structure beats clutter.

Where to find it in QuickBooks: ⚙️ Settings → Chart of Accounts
There you can: View accounts, Add accounts, Edit names, Make accounts inactive
The Chart of Accounts is strictly your financial structure.
---
Subaccounts are used when you need more detail under a main account — without cluttering your financial statements at the top 
level. In QuickBooks Online, a subaccount sits under a “parent” account in the Chart of Accounts.
Think of it like folders inside a folder.

Example of a Parent Account:
	Utilities (Expense)
	Subaccounts:
		Electricity, Water, Internet, Gas

On your Profit & Loss:
	If collapsed → shows just Utilities
	If expanded → shows the breakdown

*That’s the purpose: detail without chaos. Use Subaccounts when you want reporting detail, but summary totals still matter.

When You SHOULD NOT Use Subaccounts
If every expense has its own account, you’re overbuilding.

When Classes or Locations are better
If you’re tracking: Departments, Job costing, Multiple business units then use Classes or Locations, not subaccounts.
Subaccounts track type. Classes track who/where.

Use subaccounts when:
The detail supports decision-making, but the summary still matters. If you never look at the breakdown, don’t create it.

Classes (Operational Tracking)
Classes track: Departments, Divisions, Locations, Service lines, Jobs (sometimes).
Classes do NOT change the Chart of Accounts. They layer tracking on top of accounts.

Example:
You have one account: Advertising Expense
But you want to know:
	Marketing Department spend
	Sales Department spend
	Admin Department spend

Instead of creating:
	Advertising – Marketing
	Advertising – Sales
	Advertising – Admin

You use:
	Class = Marketing / Sales / Admin
Now one expense account. Multiple operational views.
Use Classes when you want to indicate which part of the business generated or used the money.

If you're organizing types of money, use subaccounts.
If you're organizing parts of the business, use classes.
*Can ask AI how to redesign your setup if it’s already overbuilt.

Definitions_

Retained earnings are the cumulative net earnings or profits a company keeps after paying dividends to shareholders. 
Dividends are the last financial obligations paid by a company during a period. “Retained” refers to the fact that 
those earnings were kept by the company.

Companies may use their retained earnings to increase production capacity, hire more employees, launch a new product, 
or for share buybacks, among other uses.
---
Categories (a.k.a. Account Types or Buckets) are broad groupings used to organize financial information on the financial 
statements. Categories are the high-level classifications that determine how accounts are grouped and reported in financial 
statements.
Examples of categories: Assets, Liabilities, Equity, Income, Cost of Goods Sold, Expenses
What categories do:
Determine where something appears on reports
Control how totals roll up on the Balance Sheet or Profit & Loss
Provide structure for your chart of accounts
---
Accounts are the specific places where transactions are recorded.
Accounts are assigned to categories
Examples of accounts: Checking Account, Accounts Receivable, Retained Earnings, Tithes & Offerings Income, 
Office Supplies Expense, Depreciation Expense

What accounts do:
	Track actual activity
	Hold balances
	Appear line by line on reports

Every account must belong to one category.

How Classes Work in the Chart of Accounts
1. What “Classes” Are
In accounting systems, classes are a tracking tool used alongside the chart of accounts—not a replacement for it. While the 
chart of accounts (COA) shows what the transaction is (expense, revenue, asset, liability), classes show why or where the 
transaction applies.
Think of it this way:
	Chart of Accounts = “What type of money is this?”
	(Rent expense, Donations income, Utilities expense)
	Classes = “Which program, department, fund, or activity does this belong to?”


2. Classes vs. the Chart of Accounts
The chart of accounts is organized by account type: Assets, Liabilities, Equity, Income, Expenses

Classes sit on top of the chart of accounts as an additional reporting dimension.
📌 Key point:
A single account (e.g., “Office Supplies Expense”) can be used across multiple classes, allowing detailed reporting without 
exploding the Chart of Accounts into hundreds of accounts.

Balance_Sheet_Explained

A balance sheet is one of the core financial statements you’ll work with as a bookkeeper, and once you understand its logic, 
it becomes a powerful tool for assessing the health of any business.

---

# **What a Balance Sheet Is**

A **balance sheet** is a financial statement that shows a company’s **financial position at a specific point in time**.  
It answers the question: *“What does the business own, what does it owe, and what’s left over for the owner?”*

It’s built on the fundamental accounting equation:  Assets = Liabilities + Equity

### **Assets**  
Everything the business owns or controls that has value.  
Examples: cash, accounts receivable, equipment, inventory.
An asset in accounting is a resource owned or controlled by a business that is expected to provide future economic benefits.

### **Liabilities**  
Everything the business owes to others.  
Examples: credit cards, loans, unpaid bills.

### **Equity**  
The owner’s residual interest after liabilities are subtracted from assets.  
Examples: owner contributions, retained earnings, net income.

---

# **The Purpose of the Balance Sheet**

The balance sheet exists to give a clear, snapshot‑style view of a company’s financial strength. Its main purposes are:

## **1. Show Financial Health**
It reveals whether the business is stable, over‑leveraged, or cash‑poor.  
For example:
- Lots of assets and low debt → strong position  
- High debt and low cash → risky position  

## **2. Evaluate Liquidity**
It helps answer: *“Can the business pay its bills?”*  
You compare short‑term assets (like cash) to short‑term liabilities (like credit cards or vendor bills).

## **3. Track Long‑Term Stability**
It shows how the business is funded:
- through debt (liabilities)  
- through owner investment and profits (equity)

This matters for lenders, investors, and tax professionals.

## **4. Support Decision‑Making**
Owners use it to decide:
- whether they can afford new equipment  
- whether they need financing  
- whether they’re growing or shrinking over time  

## **5. Provide a Foundation for Ratios**
Many financial ratios come from the balance sheet:
- current ratio  
- debt‑to‑equity  
- working capital  

These ratios help assess risk and performance.

---

# **Why This Matters for You as a Bookkeeper**

A clean balance sheet is the backbone of accurate books.  
If the balance sheet is wrong, **nothing else is right** — not the P&L, not the tax return, not the cash‑flow picture.

Your technical, systems‑driven mindset is perfect for this because:
- it’s all about structure  
- it’s all about accuracy  
- it’s all about reconciling data  

If you want, I can walk you through how to *read* a balance sheet, how to *build* one, or how to *spot errors* in one — all skills that make you a standout bookkeeper.

Balance_Sheet_Proving

The balance sheet provides a snapshot of the client’s assets, liabilities, and equity at a specific point in time. Proving the 
balance sheet is an important period-end task which involves working through each row, verifying that the data within it is 
accurate, and ensuring it matches information held in other reports and statements. When this is done, you can be confident that 
the financial records are accurate and complete.

To prove the bank balances on the balance sheet, check against the relevant bank reconciliations. 
--
To prove the accounts receivable amounts in the balance sheet, run an Accounts receivable aging summary report and confirm the 
figures match. 

The Accounts receivable aging summary report also provides a good opportunity for conversations with your client: 
If the report shows credit balances, check to see why. It could be that the client owes their customer money, or the customer 
has paid for a product or service but hasn’t been invoiced. 

If there are any invoices that need chasing down, you can change the date range on the Accounts Receivable (A/R) Aging Summary 
Report to see if any invoices have been paid since period end to see if this is necessary.

If there is anything in the report that has remained unpaid for a long time, it might be a good idea to discuss with the client 
how to handle it. If it’s considered that the amount will never be paid, leaving it in accounts receivable will mean the client’s 
books are not an accurate reflection of their business.
--
To prove the accounts payable amounts in the balance sheet, run an Accounts Payable Aging Summary report and confirm the
figures match. There may also be additional advisory conversations that you can have with your clients here too. They may value 
some coaching on best practice for terms with their customers, their collection processes, and setting up workflows to chase 
unpaid invoices.  
--
Other Current Assets
The Other Current Assets section of the balance sheet shows any other current asset accounts from the chart of accounts. 
--
Inventory Asset shows the value of inventory that the client has on hand. This is important to track as it can be a significant asset 
for many businesses, and it can also have tax implications.
The value against Inventory Asset can be checked against the Inventory Valuation Detail Report to ensure they show the same amount. 
--
Undeposited Funds (sometimes called Payments to deposit) shows the value of payments that have been received but have not yet 
been deposited into the client’s bank account. It’s important to keep track of undeposited funds and make sure that it doesn’t 
grow each period, as that would indicate that these amounts either aren’t being deposited, or have not correctly been recorded
in QuickBooks. 
--
Uncategorized Assets
If anything has been added to the Uncategorized assets account, it will show up on the balance sheet. Transactions should only 
be held in this account temporarily, and so this balance should be zero. If anything does appear in this area, find these 
transactions and reclassify them to the correct accounts. 
--
Fixed assets
Fixed assets are long-term assets that a business uses in its operations, such as equipment, vehicles, or buildings.
The value of any fixed assets will appear on the balance sheet. A periodic review of the transactions in these accounts is 
helpful to determine if a fixed asset schedule needs to be updated, or if anything was coded incorrectly, in preparation for 
year end. 
--
On the balance sheet, assets are shown at their book value, which is the original cost of the asset minus any accumulated depreciation. 
If there are any impairments or write-downs, these should also be reflected in the asset's book value. An impairment occurs when 
the asset's market value drops below its book value, and a write-down is an accounting entry that reduces the book value of the 
asset to reflect this decrease in value.

Assets are listed in order of liquidity, which means how quickly they can be converted into cash. Liquid assets, such as accounts 
receivable or inventory, can be turned into cash quickly. Equipment and buildings that take longer to sell or convert are considered 
illiquid. 
* Illiquid assets are securities or properties that you can't easily sell or exchange for cash without incurring significant losses.

There are two main types of assets:
	Current assets: These are assets you expect your business to use, sell, or convert to cash within a year, such as inventory.
	Non-current assets: These assets have longer-term value to your business and are harder to convert into cash. Examples 
	include office furniture, buildings, or long-term investments.

Say you run a small gardening business. Your current assets include cash, inventory (plants and gardening supplies), and accounts 
receivable (invoices sent to customers for work completed). Your non-current assets include your gardening equipment, such as 
lawnmowers and trimmers. The current assets are more liquid because you can quickly sell inventory or collect accounts receivable 
to get cash. The gardening equipment is less liquid because it may take time to find a buyer and convert it into cash.

Note: You should always follow best practices for treatment of fixed assets. For clients with QuickBooks Advanced subscriptions, 
you can add their fixed assets, and their depreciation schedules can be managed within QuickBooks itself.
--
Accounts Payable
The accounts payable figure in the balance sheet should be checked against the value in the Accounts payable aging summary 
report, and any statements received from vendors. 

Like the A/R Aging Summary Report, the A/P Aging Summary Report can provide a good opportunity for conversations with your client: 
	Confirm that all the balances are accurate—and determine what to do with those that aren’t 
	Discuss cash flow in relation to outstanding accounts payable

Other Current Liabilities
Any accounts in the Other Current Liabilities section should be reconciled and compared to any detailed reports or future payments. 
For example, you should be able to compare the balance in any Sales Taxes Payable accounts to future payments made or amounts that 
show due on the sales tax returns.

Similarly, if your clients have any long-term liabilities, they should be regularly reconciled against their loan documents or 
amortization schedules. * An amortization schedule is a table that shows how a loan gets paid off over time, breaking each 
payment into: Interest paid, Principal paid and Remaining loan balance.
--
Opening Balance Equity account
An entry to the Opening Balance Equity account is generated whenever an account is set up within QuickBooks with an opening 
balance. This can be useful when you’re setting up an account, but it shouldn’t be left like this, as it does not explain where 
this amount has come from.

If there is an amount in Opening Balance Equity at the end of a period, it should be investigated and resolved. Open the 
account register from the chart of accounts to view the transactions entered since the last time it was zero.

In the case of a new bank account (for example Account B), if you know that the opening balance was transferred from an existing 
account (Account A), you can simply change the account from opening balance equity to the source bank account (Account A).

In other cases, correcting opening balance equity may require more complex journal entries to ensure that the source of the 
opening balance is properly reported in QuickBooks.

Profit_And_Loss_Checking

Checking the profit and loss statement
You should take a look at the profit and loss statement and check it for reasonableness. Some issues will be easier to spot than 
others, for example, if there is a balance in Uncategorized Income. Others will require more of your accountancy detective 
skills. You should review the profit and loss statement to check that the expense and income account balances make sense for 
your client.

Note: if your client is a nonprofit organization, this report may be called the statement of activities.

Sales income is specifically related to invoices and sales receipts.
It can be a good idea to tie out the sales income account balance using items in the profit and loss against the sales summary 
report. If there is a difference between the sales summary report and the profit and loss, make sure that any nonsales transactions 
are recategorized to a different income account.
---
Uncategorized income accounts are generated by QuickBooks when there isn’t an obvious match for a money-in transaction. If these 
Uncategorized income transactions are not recategorized to a more relevant account, then these accounts will show up on the profit
and loss statement. If you see a balance in these accounts on the profit and loss statement:
	Run a Profit and Loss Detail Report. 
	Select each transaction in turn and recategorize it to the correction account.

You may need to work with your client to identify the correct accounts for each transaction. After client discussions, it’s a good 
idea to update the rules in the bank feed, to avoid further transitions being posted to uncategorized income.
---
As with Uncategorized Income, you should work with your client to reclassify any transactions posted to Uncategorized Expenses to 
avoid this account appearing on the profit and loss statement. 

If there are many transactions posted to an Uncategorized Account and they all need reposting to the same account, it might be a 
good idea to use the Reclassify transactions tool, which allows you to repost transactions in bulk. 
You should also consider if any rules in the bank feed need updating to avoid future issues.
---
If your client uses subaccounts in their chart of accounts, it’s useful to check how these are being used by looking at the profit 
and loss report. When using subaccounts, it’s best practice to only categorize transactions to the subaccounts, as it makes it 
clearer where money is being spent. If you see anything assigned to the parent account in the profit and loss report, you may want 
to find those transactions and reclassify them, or consider whether a new subaccount is needed.

(While less common, this is also something to look out for on the balance sheet.)
---
If you see any transactions categorized to accounts that are not relevant to your client’s business, you should work with your 
client to reclassify those transactions to the appropriate accounts.
---
You may see a line item in the Other Expenses section of the profit and loss report called Reconciliation Discrepancy. This 
feature is specific to QuickBooks, and it’s important to know what it is in case it comes up in your client’s accounts. 

The Reconciliation Discrepancy line on a Profit and Loss (P&L) report appears when someone changes or deletes transactions after 
a bank or credit card account has already been reconciled. Its purpose is to force the books to stay balanced when a previously 
reconciled transaction is altered.
---
When a bank account is reconciled, if it does not balance, it is possible to force the reconciliation. Any difference between 
the bank statements and the data in QuickBooks will be accounted for in the Reconciliation Discrepancy account. There may be 
times when this is necessary, but it isn’t best practice, so if you see this in a profit and loss statement, you should investigate.

If the discrepancy can not be found, or it is not suitable to fix it through undoing a reconciliation on closed books, then you 
need to address the balance so it is no longer sitting in the Reconciliation Discrepancy account.

In order to clear this account, you will need to create a journal entry to move the balance to a more suitable account, such as 
Bank fees. If you find that the Reconciliation Discrepancy Account is often used, it’s a good opportunity for training your 
clients and your team in best practice, to avoid similar situations in the future. 
---
QuickBooks will automatically generate certain accounts when specific transactions or settings occur in QuickBooks. 

Two of these auto generated accounts are Billable Expense Markup and Billable Expense Income. These accounts will be created 
if your client passes the cost of certain expenses and items onto their customers. If this is the case, then you may see 
Billable Expense Markup and Billable Expense Income in their profit and loss statement. These accounts are generated if the 
relevant settings are checked in the Expenses tab of Account and settings.

If you see balances in Billable Expense Markup and Billable Expense Income accounts, check that they are correct, and applied 
to customers if needed. 
---
During period-end reviews, you should check that the wage and payroll information held in QuickBooks matches that held in your 
payroll system. At year end, you should make sure that the information on the Profit and Loss statement regarding wages and 
payroll matches the information that has been recorded on your client's tax returns. This includes the totals that were paid 
to employees and contractors (which should match the totals across all W-9 and 1099 forms respectively), and the tax accounts. 

You should compare wages and Payroll Tax accounts to the Payroll Returns, to make sure they agree. If you can’t do this, 
someone may have incorrectly posted transactions to one of those accounts. If the Wages and Payroll Tax accounts don't agree 
with filed payroll returns, run a transaction detail report for either the Wage or Tax account, and investigate any 
transactions that are not payroll type transactions. You should also investigate if payroll type transactions were posted to 
other accounts in the system beside Wages and Payroll accounts.
---
Making sure that you’re collecting W-9s on a regular basis will make the process of preparing 1099s a lot smoother when it gets 
to end of year. Send out requests for these in the Contractors tab of the Expenses center.
---
Unapplied payments
If your client is using accrual-basis accounting, usually meaning they have accounts receivable and/or accounts payable, there 
are some other accounts to be aware of. When your clients record a customer payment via the Receive Payment screen and don’t 
apply it to an invoice, the cash-basis Profit and Loss will report it as Unapplied Cash Payment Income.

Similarly, if there’s no bill associated with a bill payment transaction, if an expense or check is posted to accounts payable, 
or if a bill is put against a credit card, then the cash-basis Profit and Loss will report this as Unapplied Cash Bill Payment 
Expense.

Random_Notes_Accounting_Bookkeeping

Bookkeeping is about recording and organizing financial transactions.
It answers:
	What happened?
	Was it recorded correctly?
	Is everything categorized properly?
	Are the accounts reconciled?

Typical Bookkeeping Tasks
	Entering expenses and income
	Recording invoices and bills
	Reconciling bank and credit card accounts
	Maintaining the chart of accounts
	Managing accounts payable and receivable
	Cleaning up uncategorized transactions
It is operational and detail-focused.

Accounting is about analysis, interpretation, and decision-making.
---
To classify a transaction in QuickBooks Online means:
You decide what type of financial activity it is and where it belongs in your accounting system.
When you classify a transaction, you’re deciding:
	Does it hit Income?
	An Expense?
	An Asset?
	A Liability?
	Or Equity?
That decision controls:
	Your Profit & Loss
	Your Balance Sheet
	Your tax reporting
	Your financial accuracy

Example: Bank feed shows: $450 – Home Depot
Classifying means choosing:
	Repairs & Maintenance?
	Equipment (Asset)?
	Cost of Goods Sold?
	Office Supplies?
Each one changes your financial reports differently.
---
Asset, liability, and equity accounts appear on the balance sheet, all other accounts appear on the profit and loss statement.
---
Journal Entries are considered transactions because they change the financial position of the business. If something changes 
Assets, Liabilities, Equity, Income, or Expenses — it’s a transaction.

A journal entry is simply a manual way to record a transaction directly into the accounting system.
Instead of using a form like an Invoice, Bill, Expense or Check. You directly tell the system: Debit this account - Credit 
that account.
Example: You forgot to record depreciation.
	Journal entry: Debit Depreciation Expense, Credit Accumulated Depreciation.
That: Increases expense, Increases a contra-asset, Lowers net income, Changes equity.

Another Example
	You reclassify an expense that was posted incorrectly.
	Original: Office Supplies Expense $1,000
	Correction journal entry:
	Debit Equipment (Asset) $1,000
	Credit Office Supplies Expense $1,000
That changes: Expenses, Assets, Profit
---
Accounts, like fixed assets, tell you the value of things you own, like a truck or a warehouse. And liabilities tell you how 
much you owe, like on a mortgage. Equity accounts show you how much the business is worth to the owner. Equity accounts also 
help you track ways the owner invests or draws money from the business. You use equity accounts when the owner deposits money

An accrual is the recording of revenue or expense when it is earned or incurred — not when cash moves. It’s timing. Nothing 
more, nothing less. Accruals Exist because cash doesn’t tell the full story.

In accounting, "incurred" refers to an expense or obligation that has occurred and must be recorded, regardless of whether 
payment has been made. The term incurred indicates the moment when a business or individual becomes responsible for an expense 
or liability. It is recognized when the service has been delivered or the asset has been received, creating a legal and 
accounting obligation, even if cash has not yet been paid out. This concept is central to accrual accounting, where 
transactions are recorded at the time they occur rather than when payment is made. 

You can:
Earn revenue today and get paid next month. Incur an expense today and pay it later.
If you only record cash, your financial statements become misleading. Accrual accounting fixes that.
Revenue Accrual (Earned but Not yet Paid)
Example: You complete $5,000 worth of work in February. Customer pays in March.
Under accrual accounting:
	February
	Revenue ↑ $5,000
	Accounts Receivable ↑ $5,000
You record it when earned, instead of when it is collected.

When cash arrives in March:
	Cash ↑
	Accounts Receivable ↓
No new revenue is recorded in March.

Expense Accrual (Incurred but Not yet Paid)
Example: You receive a $2,000 invoice for services rendered in February, due in March.
Under accrual accounting:
	February
	Expenses ↑ $2,000
	Accounts Payable ↑ $2,000
You record it when incurred, instead of when it is paid.

When cash is paid in March:
	Cash ↓
	Accounts Payable ↓
No new expenses are recorded in March.

Common Types of Accruals
	Unpaid wages
	Earned but unbilled revenue
	Interest expense
	Utilities incurred but not yet billed
	Year-end tax liabilities
These are usually recorded via journal entries.

Why Accruals Matter
Without accruals:
	Profit swings wildly month to month.
	You can look profitable while actually losing money.
	Financial statements don’t reflect reality.
Accruals smooth timing differences so performance reflects actual activity.
An accrual is simply recognizing financial activity when it happens, not when cash changes hands.

	Debit Deferred Revenue $1,000
	Credit Revenue $1,000
Revenue is recognized gradually.

What Accounts Receivable Means
Accounts Receivable (A/R) is the money that customers owe your business for goods or services that you already provided but 
haven’t been paid for yet.

It’s an asset on your balance sheet because it represents future cash inflows. When you create an invoice in QuickBooks, it 
automatically increases your accounts receivable balance. When the customer pays, it decreases accounts receivable and increases 
cash. Managing accounts receivable is crucial for maintaining healthy cash flow. It is recorded as an asset on the balance sheet 
because the business expects to receive that money.

You want to make sure you’re invoicing promptly, following up on overdue payments, and keeping track of who owes you what.
If you send a client an invoice for $2,000, Accounts Receivable increases $2,000 and the invoice appears on the A/R Aging Report. 
When the client pays the invoice, Cash increases $2,000 and Accounts Receivable decreases $2,000.

An asset in accounting is a resource owned or controlled by a business that is expected to provide future economic benefits.

Assets are valuable because they can generate revenue or be converted into cash. Assets are a key component of a company’s net 
worth. Lenders may also factor in a company’s assets when issuing loans.

Assets can be physical items, such as machinery, or intangible things, such as intellectual property. Assets are reported on a 
company’s balance sheet. A business cannot operate effectively or sustain itself without assets; they are critical to producing 
and marketing goods or services, which is essential for generating revenue and profit.

Assets are important to informed decision-making. Knowing which assets a business has and their condition can drive choices 
around upgrades, investments, resource allocation, and strategic planning. Finally, asset management is key to transparent 
financial reporting and regulatory compliance, keeping a company on the right side of key stakeholders who have a vested 
interest in its financial stance.

Examples: Accounts Receivable, Inventory, Equipment, Cash, Intellectual Property, Patents(Intangible asset), Prepaid Expenses.

Getting ready for 1099 preparation
The Books review center steps you through the core tasks that are a good idea to do at period end, but there are other things

also appear on the Contractors list. If you see a vendor on the list that should be tracked for 1099, open up their details, 
select Edit, then check Track payments for 1099.

If no W-9 information is held for the contractor, you’ll see a W-9 missing flag by their name, making it easier to identify who 
needs to be contacted for a W-9.

Every journal entry has at least one debit and one credit, and the total debits must equal total credits. This is the 
fundamental principle of double-entry accounting. It ensures that the accounting equation (Assets = Liabilities + Equity) 
remains balanced after every transaction.

How do Debits and Credits Work Together?
Debits and credits are the two sides of every financial transaction. In double-entry accounting, every debit must have a 
corresponding credit, and vice versa. This system helps ensure accuracy and provides a complete picture of a company's 
financial activities.

Owners equity changes on three main things: Revenue, Expenses, Dividends, and Owner’s Draws. When you record revenue, it increases 
equity. When you record an expense, pay a dividend, or take an owner’s draw, it decreases equity.

Step 3: Introduce Your Service Clearly

Now explain your management reporting in plain language.

Example script:

“What I provide isn’t just bookkeeping. Each month, I prepare a structured management report that shows your Profit & Loss, balance sheet, cash position, receivables, payables, and key performance metrics. But more importantly, I include a summary explaining what changed and what needs attention.”

Keep it simple. No accounting jargon.

Step 4: Emphasize Outcomes

Shift from features to impact:

“The goal is that you never wonder where the business stands. You’ll know if margins are shrinking, if expenses are creeping up, or if cash flow is tightening — before it becomes a problem.”

That’s what they’re buying.

Clarity.
Predictability.
Control.

Step 5: Explain the Monthly Review Call

This is where the value really lands.

“We also do a monthly review call where I walk you through the numbers and answer questions. That way you’re not just getting a PDF — you’re actually understanding what it means.”

That separates you from low-cost bookkeepers.

Step 6: Position It as Proactive, Not Reactive

You can say:

“Most business owners only look at numbers during tax season. By then, it’s too late to adjust strategy. My approach keeps you informed month to month.”

This subtly raises urgency without pressure.

Step 7: Handle Pricing With Confidence

If they ask why it costs more than basic bookkeeping:

“Basic bookkeeping records history. Management reporting helps you manage the future. That’s the difference.”

Simple. Direct. Confident.

Don’t over-explain pricing. That weakens positioning.

Step 8: Close With a Soft Commitment

End with something like:

“Would having that level of financial visibility each month be valuable for you?”

Let them say yes.

Then:

“Great — I’ll outline the package and next steps.”

What NOT To Do

Don’t:

Over-explain accounting theory

Talk about software features

Dump report names without context

Apologize for pricing

Sound unsure

Confidence sells advisory services.

Short Version (If You Need a Tight Script)

If you want a condensed 30-second explanation:

“I provide monthly management reporting, which means you get clean books, structured financial reports, and a review call where we go over what the numbers mean. The goal is simple — no surprises, clear visibility, and informed decisions.”

Clean. Professional. Direct.
