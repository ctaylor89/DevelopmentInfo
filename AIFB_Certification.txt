Context: AIPB Certified Bookkeeper Course  Output: What subjects and concepts are covered in this course?

The American Institute of Professional Bookkeepers (AIPB) Certified Bookkeeper (CB) course is designed to prepare working bookkeepers 
for the national certification exam. It focuses on advanced bookkeeping skills, not beginner bookkeeping.

The course curriculum is organized into six major subject areas, each taught through a workbook or module.

Subjects Covered in the AIPB Certified Bookkeeper Course
1. Adjusting Entries (Accrual Accounting)

This section teaches how to properly adjust financial records at the end of an accounting period.
Key concepts include:
	Accruals vs. deferrals
	Accrued revenue and accrued expenses
	Unearned revenue (revenue collected in advance)
	Prepaid expenses
	End-of-period adjustments
	Preparing an adjusted trial balance
	Converting trial balances into financial statements

Why it matters: These adjustments ensure financial statements follow accrual accounting rules and accurately reflect 
the company’s financial position.

2. Correction of Accounting Errors
This unit focuses on detecting and correcting mistakes in the accounting records.
Topics include:
	Identifying common bookkeeping errors
	Using the trial balance to detect errors
	Bank reconciliation procedures
	Correcting accrual and deferral errors
	Recording correcting journal entries

Why it matters:
	Error correction is a core skill because mistakes happen frequently in real bookkeeping systems.

3. Payroll Accounting
This section teaches how to run payroll and handle employment taxes.
Concepts include:
Employees vs independent contractors
Wage calculations and overtime rules
Payroll deductions and withholdings
Employer payroll taxes
Payroll journal entries
Federal forms such as:
	W-4
	941
	940
	W-2
	W-3

Why it matters:
	Payroll compliance is highly regulated and mistakes can lead to tax penalties.

4. Depreciation (Book and Tax)
This unit explains how businesses account for long-term assets.
Topics include:
	Depreciating buildings, equipment, and vehicles
	GAAP depreciation methods:
	Straight-line
	Declining balance
	Units of production
	Sum-of-the-years’ digits
	Depreciation differences between book accounting and tax reporting

Why it matters:
	Depreciation affects both financial statements and taxes, so accuracy is critical.

5. Inventory Accounting
This module covers how companies record and value inventory.
Topics include:
	Periodic vs perpetual inventory systems
	Recording inventory purchases and sales
	Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)
	Inventory valuation methods:
	FIFO
	LIFO
	Weighted average
	Moving average
	Lower of Cost or Net Realizable Value (LCNRV) rule

Why it matters:
	Inventory valuation directly affects profit and tax reporting.

6. Internal Controls and Fraud Prevention
This final section focuses on protecting company assets and detecting fraud.
Concepts include:
	Segregation of duties
	Cash handling controls
	Fraud red flags
	Preventing employee theft
	Preventing vendor fraud
	Preventing credit card and check fraud
	Security controls in accounting systems

Why it matters:
Good internal controls protect businesses from financial loss and embezzlement.

✅ Simple way to remember the curriculum:
The AIPB certification focuses on six advanced bookkeeping skill areas:
	Adjusting Entries
	Error Correction
	Payroll
	Depreciation
	Inventory
	Internal Controls & Fraud Prevention

Important insight:
The course assumes you already know basic bookkeeping (debits/credits, journals, ledgers, financial statements). It is 
designed to upgrade a bookkeeper to “full-charge bookkeeper” level skills rather than teach accounting from scratch.

Revenue is the amount a company earns from selling goods and/or providing services to its customers and clients. It is the top 
line of the income(P&L) statement and a key indicator of business performance. Revenue can come from various sources, such as product 
sales, service fees, interest income, and rental income.
---
An expense in accounting is a cost incurred to generate revenue or a reduction in the value of an asset used in business 
operations, recorded on the income statement to determine net income.

An expense is a cost that is used up, consumed, or incurred in the process of earning revenue during the current accounting period.
Key Characteristics are:
	An expense reduces net income on the Profit & Loss (Income Statement).
	An expense represents a cost that has already provided its benefit (unlike an asset, which provides future benefit).
	An expense is recorded when incurred, not necessarily when paid (accrual basis).

Examples: Rent, utilities, office supplies used up, wages, insurance expense (portion that applies to the current period).

---
Cost Definition: A general term meaning the amount paid (or owed) to acquire something.

Important: A cost is not automatically an expense.
A cost becomes either an asset (if it provides future benefit), an expense (once the benefit is used up).

Examples:
	Cost of a computer (initially an asset)
	Cost of supplies (asset until used)
	Cost of inventory (asset until sold)

Cost simply means the amount paid (or incurred) to obtain something. What that cost becomes in the accounting records depends 
on how and when the benefit is used.
A cost can end up as an asset, an expense, or a liability or even a revenue, depending on the circumstances. For example:
	Cost of a computer → Asset (until used up through depreciation)
	Cost of supplies → Asset (until used up, then becomes an expense)
	Cost of inventory → Asset (until sold, then becomes COGS expense)
	Cost of services → Expense (immediately consumed)
	Cost incurred but not yet paid → Liability (accrued expense)

The acronym COGS stands for Cost of Goods Sold. It represents the direct costs attributable to the production of the goods sold 
by a company. This includes the cost of materials and labor directly used to create the product. COGS is an expense because it 
reflects the cost of inventory that has been sold during the period, which has been used up to generate revenue.

A COGS expense means the cost of inventory that has been sold during the period. It is an expense because the inventory has been 
used up to generate revenue. These classifications follow rules like the Matching Principle.

---
Asset Definition:  
Asset is a type of cost that provides future economic benefit beyond the current accounting period.
Key points:
	Assets appear on the Balance Sheet
	Assets becomes an expense over time (depreciation, amortization, usage)
	Examples:
		Equipment
		Prepaid insurance
		Inventory
		Supplies on hand
		Buildings
		Vehicles

AIPB questions often test this logic:
	If the benefit is future → Asset.
	If the benefit is current → Expense.

Common adjusting entries:

An accrued expense is an expense that a business has already incurred but has not yet paid or recorded by the end of the accounting period.
	Accrued expense = you owe money. Deferred revenue = you owe work

An accrued expense is an expense that a business has already incurred but has not yet paid or recorded by the end of the accounting 
period. Under the Matching Principle, expenses must be recorded in the period in which they occur, even if the payment happens 
later. Because the business owes money, accrued expenses create a liability.

Under the Matching Principle, expenses must be recorded in the period in which they occur, even if the payment happens later. 
Because the business owes money, accrued expenses create a liability.
Example: wages owed to employees at month end.
---
Accrued revenue – revenue earned but not yet received
Example: services completed but not yet billed.
---
Deferred revenue – cash received before earning it. Deferred revenue is a liability because the business owes the customer something.
For example: prepaid service contracts.
Deferred revenue (also called unearned revenue) occurs when a business receives payment before it has delivered the product or 
service. Under the Revenue Recognition Principle, revenue can only be recorded when it is earned, not when cash is received. 
Until the service or product is delivered, the money is recorded as a liability because the business still owes the customer 
something. 

Example: if a customer pays $1,000 in advance for a service contract, the business would record a debit to the cash account for 
$1,000 and a credit to deferred revenue for $1,000. As the business delivers the service over time, it would make adjusting 
entries to recognize the revenue earned and reduce the deferred revenue account accordingly.

Cash received before work is done = Liability (Deferred Revenue)
Work performed = Revenue
---
Prepaid expenses – expenses paid in advance
Example: prepaid insurance.

A prepaid expense happens when a business pays cash before the expense occurs.
In other words:
	Cash paid first
	Expense happens later
Because the business has not yet used the benefit, the payment is recorded as an asset.
Example: A company pays $1,200 for one year of insurance.

Asset definition in accounting and finance
An asset is defined as a resource owned by an individual or organization that provides economic value. This includes physical 
properties such as inventory, vehicles, and buildings, monetary resources such as cash, investments, and receivables, as well 
as intangible properties like software and patents. Assets are recorded on a company's balance sheet and are essential for 
revenue generation and financial stability. Assets can be classified into current assets (expected to be converted to cash 
within one year) and fixed assets (long-term assets that cannot be easily converted to cash). Proper asset management is crucial 
for accurate financial reporting and informed business decision-making.
---
Key exam concept:
Bookkeepers must apply the Matching Principle, which means expenses are recorded in the same period as the revenue they helped generate.

Key Principles
Revenue Recognition Principle: Revenue is recorded when it is earned, even if payment has not yet been received. For example, if 
a company completes a $5,000 project in December but receives payment in January, the revenue is recognized in December. 

Matching Principle: Expenses are recorded in the same period as the revenues they help generate. This ensures that financial statements 
reflect the true profitability of a period. For instance, if a company incurs utility costs in December but pays the invoice in January, 
the expense is recorded in December. 

---
Common bookkeeping errors to avoid:
1. Transposition errors: writing numbers in the wrong order (e.g., $123 instead of $132).
2. Omission errors: forgetting to record a transaction.
3. Duplication errors: recording the same transaction more than once.
4. Misclassification errors: recording a transaction in the wrong account (e.g., recording a capital expenditure as an expense).
5. Timing errors: recording a transaction in the wrong accounting period.
